SpaceX Bought 18% of Tesla Cybertrucks in Q4 2025

If you feel like the lines between Elon Musk’s various companies are getting blurry, you aren’t alone. It seems like every time you hear about a major product success for one of his ventures, there’s a catch—or, more specifically, an internal transaction.

When data surfaced in April 2026 showing that SpaceX had purchased a massive chunk of Tesla’s Cybertruck inventory, the internet erupted. To many observers, this wasn’t just corporate synergy; it looked like a classic case of shifting money from one pocket to another to manufacture the illusion of demand. Personally, I find the whole “revolving door of capital” strategy pretty exhausting to track, but it’s clearly become the new business model.

Regulators, shareholders, and legal experts are suddenly paying close attention for a reason. Here is what is actually happening behind the scenes with the “Cyberbase.”

The Numbers Behind the Buzz#

To understand the frustration, look at the hard data from S&P Global Mobility. Their April 2026 report on Q4 2025 U.S. registration data tells a revealing story.

In those three months, there were 7,071 Cybertruck registrations total. But here’s the kicker: SpaceX bought 1,279 of them. That one company accounted for 18.09% of every Cybertruck registered in the entire country. Honestly, that’s a massive slice of the pie for a single customer.

When you add in the other entities under Musk’s umbrella, the concentration gets even tighter:

  • SpaceX: 1,279 units
  • The Boring Company: ~25 units
  • xAI: ~20 units
  • Neuralink: ~15 units
  • Total Musk-owned purchases: 1,339 units (roughly 19% of all U.S. registrations)

If you pull these internal purchases out of the equation, the Cybertruck’s market performance looks grim. Total U.S. registrations for the truck hit 14,430 in Q4 2024. By Q4 2025, that number cratered. Once you strip away the fleet purchases made by Musk’s own companies, year-over-year registrations plummeted by 51%.

Why This Matters: The “Self-Sustaining Economy” Problem#

When those numbers leaked, the internet did what it does best: it turned them into memes. People immediately started spamming a famous line from It’s Always Sunny in Philadelphia: “Thus creating the self-sustaining economy we’ve been looking for.”

Funny as that reference is, the actual financial reality is pretty grim. In business circles, this is called “round-tripping.”

It’s a simple shell game. A company sells a product to a partner or a related entity, and that entity pays up, which artificially inflates the seller’s revenue. If Tesla sells a truck to SpaceX, Tesla books that as a win on their quarterly earnings. Meanwhile, SpaceX just uses its own cash to buy the thing. To an outsider, it looks like a legitimate sale to a customer, but the money never actually left the Musk ecosystem. It’s just moving from one pocket to another.

Honestly, it’s a sloppy way to cook the books.

That’s exactly why the Department of Justice and the SEC are breathing down their necks, digging into the “related party transactions” between Tesla and Musk’s other ventures.

The Legal Heat: “Project 42” and Beyond#

Scrutiny isn’t limited to just Twitter memes. Investigators are currently deep into “Project 42,” a probe looking into whether Tesla funds were funneled into Musk’s personal ventures.

This data now sits at the center of a major lawsuit in Delaware. The Cleveland Bakers and Teamsters Pension Fund argues that Musk is treating Tesla, SpaceX, xAI, and The Boring Company like a shared piggy bank, at the expense of Tesla shareholders. Honestly, it’s a messy situation that makes you wonder where one company ends and the next begins.

The plaintiffs aren’t asking for pocket change. They want Musk to transfer his xAI equity—worth about $24 billion—directly to Tesla to make up for the resources he allegedly siphoned off.

The Counter-Argument: Operational Necessity#

Wes Morrill, Tesla’s Lead Engineer for the Cybertruck, made a simple point on X: “fleet replacement was always part of the dream.”

From that perspective, it makes total sense for SpaceX to stock their facilities with Cybertrucks. Honestly, I’d be shocked if they didn’t. If your own company builds a utility vehicle, buying a competitor’s truck for your fleet would be a bizarre business move. Keeping the money in-house is smart, and it forces those trucks to face real-world testing every single day.

But the scale here is wild. SpaceX dropping over $100 million on trucks in a single quarter is exactly what has critics and regulators raising their eyebrows. Buying a few fleet vehicles is one thing. Being the primary engine of demand that keeps the company’s sales figures from cratering is something else entirely.

To see how much these entities actually pad the bottom line, look at the 2025-2026 breakdown:

The “organic” market—real people buying trucks without a connection to Musk’s companies—is much smaller than the headline numbers suggest. Honestly, it’s a bit jarring to see the gap. By Q1 2026, those organic registrations cratered to 3,519 units.

What Should You Think?#

Whether you’re an investor, a fan, or just watching the chaos from the sidelines, here is what you need to know:

  1. Watch the “Related Party” labels. When a press release brags about “record-breaking sales” or “strong demand,” check where that money is coming from. Are real people buying these things, or is the company just selling to itself? With the Cybertruck, that distinction completely changes how you view the company’s health. Honestly, it’s a red flag that most people are choosing to ignore.

  2. Follow the litigation. The Delaware Chancery Court case and the DOJ’s “Project 42” investigation will set a massive precedent. If the courts decide these internal deals are a breach of fiduciary duty, Musk’s entire empire faces a massive shake-up.

  3. Revenue doesn’t equal profit. Revenue is just a vanity metric. If a company generates cash by moving money from one corporate pocket to another, it isn’t a growing business—it’s just an accounting trick. That doesn’t translate to success in the real world.

A product that sells primarily to its own founder’s other companies isn’t a market triumph; it’s an experimental fleet. We’ll find out soon enough if this “self-sustaining economy” is a stroke of genius or a massive legal liability. For Tesla shareholders, the court’s verdict is easily the most important event of the year.

Keep a close eye on the upcoming earnings calls. Tesla loves to gloss over these transactions, but the pressure from regulators and shareholders means they won’t be able to dodge the “Cyberbase” questions for much longer.
