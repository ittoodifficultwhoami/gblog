The Dodgers' Digital-Only Policy Excludes Loyal Seniors

March 29, 2026 · 5 min · MinWeb

Imagine you’ve been a season ticket holder for the same team for 50 years. You were there during the Nixon administration, you weathered the losing seasons, you cheered through the championships, and you kept your seat through thick and thin. Then, at 82 years old, you’re told your loyalty doesn’t grant you one simple request: a physical piece of paper to get into the stadium.

This isn’t a hypothetical. It’s the reality for a long-time Los Angeles Dodgers fan who asked for paper tickets—even offering to pay for the printing costs—only to be told the decision was final.

When we talk about the “digital divide,” we usually picture abstract charts. But when a team that prides itself on “Dodger blue” bloodlines turns its back on an 82-year-old who literally can’t navigate a smartphone due to medical needs, that divide stops being theoretical. It’s a moral failure. Honestly, it’s just plain cruel.

The Numbers Behind the Struggle#

The Dodgers aren’t alone in pushing “contactless entry,” a trend that took off during the pandemic to cut down on staff interaction. The problem is that this shift ignores the reality of their aging fanbase.

According to AARP Tech Trends 2025, only 35% to 45% of adults aged 80 and older own a smartphone. Compare that to the 91% ownership rate for 50-year-olds, and you have a massive demographic chasm. Even worse, 59% of seniors over 70 say modern technology isn’t designed for them.

When you force an 82-year-old to use an app with “rotating barcodes”—which refresh every few seconds to prevent screenshots—you aren’t creating a “modern” experience. You’re building a wall. It’s functionally impossible for many seniors, especially those recovering from medical procedures, to navigate this.

Why Are Teams Pushing This So Hard?#

If the fans hate it, and it alienates the most loyal segment of the base, why do it? It comes down to two things: money and data.

  • Data Harvesting: Through the MLB Ballpark app, teams track exactly who you are, where you go, and what you buy. This personal data is a goldmine for marketing.
  • The “Souvenir” Monetization: By killing off paper stubs, teams found a way to charge you twice. Teams like the Texas Rangers and Atlanta Braves now sell “commemorative” physical tickets for $10 to $25. They turned a free byproduct of your entry into a new revenue stream.
  • Security Concerns: Ironically, digital hasn’t made things safer. The Lanham v. MLB Advanced Media, L.P. lawsuit, filed in September 2025, points to major security holes. The suit alleges that thousands of digital tickets were stolen because MLB failed to implement basic two-factor authentication.

A Comparison of Policies: Is There a Better Way?#

The tragedy here is that the Dodgers don’t have to do it this way. Other groups have balanced modern security with basic human compassion.

OrganizationPolicy Approach
Washington Capitals (NHL)Reintroduced physical tickets for 2025-26.

LA DodgersMaintains a “final” digital-only policy.

The term for what the Dodgers are refusing to do is “grandfathering.” It’s common sense in almost every other industry to let long-standing customers keep their service level while slowly transitioning new folks to the new way of doing things.

Legislative Failures and the Road Ahead#

You’re probably wondering why the government doesn’t step in. California tried. The bill AB 1291, which would have mandated that venues honor physical proof of purchase, failed in February 2026. Another bill, AB 1720, is currently under review but specifically excludes sports. That leaves fans at the mercy of dynamic pricing and digital-only platforms.

Without legislative protection or team-level empathy, the “digital-only” movement acts as a filter that quietly pushes older, less tech-savvy fans out of the stadium.

The Human Cost#

The public response to this story has been overwhelmingly negative. Fans are making it clear that this isn’t just about tech—it’s about a total lack of respect.

A “win” for the Dodgers would be simple: designate a window or a kiosk where seniors or those with disabilities can still access paper passes. It’s a low-cost gesture that would signal they value people over data. Instead, the organization is doubling down on a system that is prone to hacking, frustrating to use, and inherently exclusionary.

What Does This Mean for You?#

If you value tradition or just want to hold your ticket in your hand, you’re in for an uphill battle. Here is what you can do:

  1. Vote with your presence: If you belong to a fan group, make it known that accessibility for seniors is a priority for your renewal. Teams only change policy when they worry about their bottom line.
  2. Support legislation: Pay attention to consumer rights bills regarding digital access. Even when bills fail, they build momentum.
  3. Advocate for “Grandfathering”: Write to the team’s front office requesting an exception for fans who have held accounts for decades.
  4. Demand Security: If we’re forced to use these apps, demand better security. As seen in the Lanham lawsuit, teams have a moral and legal obligation to protect your account.

The Dodgers’ current policy is a sharp reminder that in modern sports, “fan experience” is often secondary to data collection. It’s a sad day for baseball when a 50-year commitment is discarded for the convenience of a rotating barcode. It’s time for fans to speak up and remind these organizations that they are in the business of serving people—not just harvesting them.
