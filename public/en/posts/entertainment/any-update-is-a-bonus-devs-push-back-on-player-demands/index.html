Any Update Is a Bonus': Devs Push Back on Player Demands

April 3, 2026 · 5 min · MinWeb
Table of Contents
  • The PEAK Phenomenon: A Brief History
  • What Have the Devs Actually Done?
  • Why the “Live Service” Expectation is Toxic
  • The Problem with “Entitlement Gaming”
  • Where Do We Go From Here?

We’re living in the era of the “forever game.” From massive online shooters to sprawling survival titles, we’ve been conditioned to expect a steady, unending drip-feed of content. If a game launches today, we assume it will be supported, expanded, and tweaked for the next decade.

But what happens when the game is actually finished? You pay a fair price for a complete experience, only to find players demanding that developers never move on to anything else. Honestly, it’s a bit exhausting to watch.

This is the exact situation unfolding with PEAK, the surprise hit from Landfall Games and Aggro Crab. Despite a massive launch and consistent updates, the developers finally drew a line in the sand. They issued a blunt statement that sent shockwaves through the community: “Any update is a bonus, not a right.”

The PEAK Phenomenon: A Brief History#

To understand this friction, look at the sheer scale of PEAK’s success. The game hit Steam on June 16, 2025, with an $8 price tag, and it became a runaway hit almost overnight.

The numbers are staggering for a title this size:

  • First 24 hours: 100,000 copies sold.
  • First 6 days: 1 million copies sold.
  • First month: 5 million copies sold.
  • By August 2025: Over 10 million copies sold.

With 120,000 Steam reviews, the game holds an “Overwhelmingly Positive” rating. It peaked at 170,000 concurrent players. Even now, in April 2026, it keeps a healthy base of 20,000 to 35,000 active users.

For eight bucks, the developers offered a massive amount of game. Honestly, I’ve spent more than that on a lukewarm sandwich. Yet, as 2026 rolled around, players started accusing the studio of a “lazy dev cycle.”

What Have the Devs Actually Done?#

Calling the dev cycle “lazy” is wild when you actually look at the work Landfall and Aggro Crab put into the game’s first year. These teams haven’t been sitting on their hands. Since the June 2025 launch, they’ve cranked out three major content updates and over 30 patches.

Here is what that support actually looked like:

Update NameDateKey Features
Mesa UpdateAugust 11, 2025New biome, items, and cosmetics
Roots UpdateNovember 5, 2025Fungal forest biome, new enemies
Play It Your WayMarch 30, 2026Custom runs, mini-runs, and autosave
April Fool’s UpdateApril 1, 2026Grapple Mode, Spartan kick mechanic

The developers clearly listened to the community—the “Play It Your Way” update alone added a massive amount of replayability. Still, the constant demands eventually pushed them to a breaking point. On April 1, 2026, they issued a blunt response to the noise: “Neither us or Aggro Crab are live service studios, any update is a bonus not a right.”

Honestly? They’re right. We’ve become way too comfortable demanding infinite content from games that were never designed to be endless treadmill experiences.

Why the “Live Service” Expectation is Toxic#

The conflict here isn’t just about a specific game—it’s a fundamental misunderstanding of what a studio actually is.

Landfall Games and Aggro Crab are small, independent teams. They aren’t massive, publicly traded corporations with thousands of employees dedicated to patching the same product until the end of time. Honestly, expecting them to operate like Ubisoft or EA is a recipe for disappointment.

Take Aggro Crab. Their history is a perfect example of why studios have to move on. They had to cancel Going Under 2 in early 2025 because the money simply ran out. That failure, and the pivot to PEAK, is the only reason we got their latest title at all. For these devs, moving on to new projects—like the Crashout Crew demo—isn’t a preference. It’s a business necessity. They move or they die.

When players demand constant, indefinite updates, they’re asking developers to stop making new games. They are forcing creative studios to become maintenance crews for software that was finished years ago.

The Problem with “Entitlement Gaming”#

Why are players so angry? It comes down to a fundamental shift in how we see digital goods. Because we mostly buy games as downloads now, we’ve tricked ourselves into thinking we’re subscribing to a service instead of just buying a product. Honestly, it’s a weird way to look at a hobby.

At $8, PEAK isn’t a subscription. It’s a one-time purchase. If you play for 15 hours and have a blast, the transaction is finished. Those extra biomes and custom run modes were bonuses—gifts from the developers to say thanks—not some contractual obligation.

Treating developers like an endless fountain of free content is unsustainable. It burns them out, forces studios to kill new projects just to maintain old ones, and creates a toxic cycle where developers stop talking to us entirely because they know they’ll just be met with more demands.

Where Do We Go From Here?#

The developers just confirmed one last major biome for late 2026. This is the end of the road for the game’s content updates, and the team is officially moving on to new projects.

For players, this is a clear sign to change how you look at releases. Here is how we should approach gaming from now on:

  1. Evaluate the value at launch. Whether a game is $8 or $60, look at what is actually in the box today. If it’s fun right now, buy it. If you’re betting on future updates that haven’t been promised, you’re just setting yourself up for a headache.
  2. Respect a finished product. A game being “done” isn’t a failure—it’s a success. It means the team had a vision and actually finished it. Honestly, more games could stand to be “done” instead of dragged out forever.
  3. Support studio growth. If you love PEAK, don’t demand endless updates. Buy their next game instead. By letting them move on to projects like Crashout Crew, you keep the studio in business so they can keep making quality stuff.

We’ve been spoiled by the “live service” model, but we have to remember there are real human beings behind every patch. They made a great game and gave us plenty of hours of fun.

Sometimes, the best move is to just say thanks and let them get to work on the next big thing.
