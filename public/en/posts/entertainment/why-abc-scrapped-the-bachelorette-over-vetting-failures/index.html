Why ABC Scrapped The Bachelorette Over Vetting Failures

ABC's decision to scrap The Bachelorette highlights a systemic failure in vetting as networks prioritize viral social media fame over basic contestant safety.

Networks like ABC don’t just flush $20 million down the drain for fun. When they pulled the plug on the 22nd season of The Bachelorette just three days before it hit your screen, it wasn’t just a scheduling change. It was a massive, expensive failure of corporate oversight that proves reality television executives prioritize viral drama over basic safety every single time.

On March 19, 2026, Disney Entertainment Television killed the season featuring Taylor Frankie Paul. They had no choice. A video surfaced from 2023 showing Paul throwing a metal barstool that hit her young child in the head. At the time, Paul was under a plea in abeyance for aggravated assault. The network knew about these legal troubles, but they moved forward anyway, betting that her internet fame would drive ratings. They lost that bet.

Why the vetting process failed#

Most viewers think stars of big network shows go through FBI-level background checks. They don’t. Industry scouts actually hunt for chaotic personalities because conflict is the easiest way to generate headlines.

Paul’s legal issues were public record. Producers knew she was serving a 36-month plea deal for a third-degree felony involving violence. They ignored the red flags because they were blinded by her Mom Tok follower count. It is a cynical business strategy: bank on the drama, hope for the best, and cross your fingers that the past stays buried. When the video of the child in distress went viral, the “bad girl” marketing angle became a brand-destroying liability that Disney couldn’t scrub away.

The cost of choosing drama over safety#

This wasn’t just an ethical mess; it was a financial disaster.

  • Average cost per episode: $2 million.
  • Total wasted production cost: $18 million to $26 million.
  • Audience reaction: Paul lost over 100,000 Instagram followers in 24 hours.
  • Ongoing legal issues: As of late February 2026, Paul was under investigation for a separate incident involving Dakota Mortensen.

Networks rely on morality clauses, but they only use them when things go public. They had the information to stop this disaster before cameras ever rolled. They chose to roll the dice and they paid for it.

The end of the Mom Tok facade#

The Taylor Frankie Paul scandal effectively kills the era of the perfect Mormon influencer. For years, this group sold an aesthetic of immaculate, suburban, faith-based family life. The reality—backed by police reports and that leaked footage—is a violent, messy contrast.

  • Brand distancing: Other influencers like Whitney Leavitt and Jen Affleck scrambled to scrub their connection to Paul to save their own sponsorships.
  • Production halts: Projects like the fifth season of The Secret Lives of Mormon Wives on Hulu were paused indefinitely because the business model relies on the image of the perfect parent. Once that image shatters, the money dries up.

What to watch for in your next show#

Networks are now under the gun to change how they do business. You can expect two major shifts. First, insurance requirements for participants will skyrocket. If a network has to pay millions in premiums to cover a liability, they will stop hiring controversial influencers altogether. Second, background checks will actually start to matter. The PR risk of a candidate’s criminal history now carries a clear, multimillion-dollar price tag.

Keep these points in mind when you turn on your favorite reality series:

  • The chaos is a product: When a show markets someone as unpredictable, they are selling you a personality trait that comes with real-world legal consequences.
  • Money moves the needle: Networks only care about advertisers. The second a star becomes a net negative for a brand, the network will dump them, no matter how much they invested in the show.
  • Question the curation: If someone’s online life looks too perfect, it is usually because they are hiding something. Social media is a highlight reel, not a documentary.

The cancellation of this Bachelorette season is a wake-up call. It signals that even in the age of bottomless content, there is a limit to how much bad behavior an audience will tolerate. Don’t confuse a manufactured influencer persona with a person’s actual character. When the mask slips, the consequences are never as neat as they look on screen.
