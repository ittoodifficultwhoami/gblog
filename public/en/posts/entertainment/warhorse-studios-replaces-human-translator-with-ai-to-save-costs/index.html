Warhorse Studios Replaces Human Translator With AI to Save Costs

March 29, 2026 · 5 min · MinWeb

It’s the kind of irony that feels scripted: a studio famous for its obsessive, historical dedication to the medieval Czech experience is now at the center of a debate about the “modern” way to cut corners.

If you are a fan of Kingdom Come: Deliverance 2 (KCD2), you probably felt a distinct disconnect recently. On one hand, you have a record-breaking hit that brought the complexity of 15th-century Bohemia to life. On the other, there is a bitter conversation about how Warhorse Studios is treating the people who actually built that world.

On March 27, 2026, news broke that Max Hejtmánek—a translator and editor who had been with Warhorse since 2022—was fired. The reason? His position was “obsolete.” Warhorse, a company that pulled in a staggering CZK 1 billion (about $43 million USD) in profit for the fiscal year ending March 2025, decided to replace human expertise with AI tools to “save finances.”

This story is about more than just one person losing a job. It’s a snapshot of a gaming industry where astronomical success doesn’t stop companies from prioritizing automated “efficiency” over the human touch.

The Financial Reality vs. The “Cost-Saving” Narrative#

To understand why this sparked such a fight, look at the numbers. We aren’t talking about an indie studio struggling to keep the lights on.

Kingdom Come: Deliverance 2 launched on February 4, 2025, and by February 2026, it had already smashed expectations with over 5 million units sold. Warhorse is thriving. The studio was doing so well in late 2025 that it actually provided a $25 million loan to its parent company, the Embracer Group-owned Plaion.

When a company that is loaning out millions of dollars calls a human translator an “obsolete” expense, it raises an obvious question: Is this really about money, or is it just about chasing a trend?

The move to replace Hejtmánek with a chatbot comes straight from the top. Studio co-founder Daniel Vávra has been vocal about his support for AI. He has spent significant time defending NVIDIA’s DLSS 5 technology, even suggesting that AI is the answer to avoiding the costs of raytracing and that it serves as a viable replacement for human-handled localization. Frankly, it’s a risky gamble on the future of gaming narrative.

The Human Cost: A “Soulful” Experience Lost?#

Max Hejtmánek’s public statement regarding his departure hit a nerve. He wrote: “Imagine pouring your soul into medieval Czech dialogue, only to be replaced by a chatbot feels like history repeating, but not in a good way.”

This strikes at the heart of what makes Kingdom Come special. When you translate a game set in the Middle Ages, you aren’t just converting words. You are managing period-accurate dialects, cultural nuances, and the “soul” of the dialogue—the weight behind the words.

AI models operate on patterns and probabilities. They lack the context of a living, breathing translator who understands the specific historical tone that Warhorse spent years cultivating.

Why the Community is Fighting Back#

The reaction on Reddit has been swift and unforgiving. The general consensus isn’t just disappointment; it’s a sense of betrayal. Players bought those 5 million copies because they wanted the handcrafted experience the studio promised.

The community’s frustration comes down to a few things:

  1. The “Efficiency” Lie: Fans are quick to point out that while Warhorse saves money by firing staff, the price of the game stays the same. The “savings” don’t help the player.
  2. The DLSS 5 Precedent: Many were already wary of the studio’s pivot to AI. When developers like Dave Oshry label some of these AI implementations as “AI slop,” it’s clear the tech-forward path Warhorse is taking isn’t welcomed by everyone in the industry.
  3. The Quality Concern: There is a fear that “non-sense gibberish” will become the norm. In a game that relies so heavily on immersion, poor localization breaks the world entirely.

As one Reddit user put it, “Everyone disliked that.”

The “Obsolescence” Trap#

The term “obsolete” is a heavy one. When a company uses it, they are saying the value of human nuance is zero—or at least, less than the cost of an API subscription.

Translation has never been about swapping word A for word B. It’s about communication. In a historical RPG, it’s about keeping the player anchored in the time period. If an AI replaces the person who ensures a peasant sounds like a peasant, the player feels the loss.

Leadership justifies this by saying they want to be “more effective.” They are defining effectiveness as capital preservation. But for players, effectiveness is measured by the quality of the story.

What Now?#

It is easy to feel cynical, but as consumers, we have a role in how this plays out.

  • Value the Credits: Pay attention to the writers and the localization teams. If a game shines because of its dialogue, it’s because of those people. Supporting titles that explicitly hire and credit humans is one way to vote with your wallet.
  • Demand Quality, Not Just Content: If a game feels like it was put through an AI filter—where the sentences feel “off” or the tone is inconsistent—call it out. Don’t be afraid to leave reviews. Companies are driven by sentiment; if the feedback is negative, they have to listen.
  • Keep Perspective: When companies brag about “making the company more effective” through automation, remind yourself who that effectiveness is for. It is for the shareholders, not for you. If you are paying $70 for a game, you have the right to expect that the narrative was overseen by a human who understands the world they are building.

The Kingdom Come: Deliverance 2 controversy is a reminder that the future of gaming is being written right now. It is a future that could use technology to enhance human creativity—or use it to strip away the things that make these worlds worth visiting. For now, the community is making it clear: they don’t want a chatbot telling them the history of Bohemia. They want the heart and soul that only a human can provide.
