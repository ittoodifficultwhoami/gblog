Why She-Hulk Is Actually One of Marvel’s Biggest Hits

April 12, 2026 · 6 min · MinWeb
Table of Contents

If you spent any time on social media during the summer of 2022, you saw the polarized discourse surrounding She-Hulk: Attorney at Law. For every viewer who found the meta-humor refreshing, there was a chorus of angry voices ready to dissect everything from the CGI to the courtroom antics.

Fast forward to April 2026, and the narrative has shifted—at least from inside Marvel Studios. Brad Winderbaum, Marvel’s Head of Streaming, recently stated on The Escape Pod that She-Hulk is “one of our best-performing shows” and “just hit the general audience.”

Wait—what? How can a show that faced such a vocal backlash be labeled a massive success? If you’re getting whiplash, you aren’t alone. I’ve been scratching my head over this one for a week. Fans want to know by what metric this show actually won. Is it just corporate spin, or is there hard data hiding beneath the surface? Let’s pull back the curtain on the streaming business to see why She-Hulk is both a major victory and a cautionary tale for Marvel.

The “Numbers” Game: Why Metrics Matter#

To understand why Winderbaum calls She-Hulk a success, you have to ignore the noise on Twitter and look at how streaming platforms actually keep score.

When Nielsen drops its ratings, they focus on “minutes watched.” This is exactly where She-Hulk started at a disadvantage. Because the episodes run about 30 minutes—a stark contrast to the 50-minute blocks of Loki or WandaVision—the math was rigged against it from the start. You just can’t rack up as many minutes watching a half-hour comedy as you can with a sprawling hour-long drama. Honestly, it’s a miracle the show held its own at all.

Despite that hurdle, the show performed steadily. Here is how it stacked up in U.S. households during its four-day premiere window, according to data from Samba TV:

She-Hulk sits right in the middle of the pack. It isn’t the juggernaut that Loki was, but it easily outperformed other highly anticipated titles like Secret Invasion. During the 2022 Disney Q4 earnings call, former CEO Bob Chapek explicitly cited She-Hulk and Ms. Marvel as key drivers for keeping subscribers glued to the platform.

The “Budget Trap”#

If the show performed well with audiences, why does it feel like Disney is sweeping it under the rug? The answer is simple: the price tag.

The production budget for She-Hulk hit roughly $25 million per episode. Across nine episodes, that puts the total at $225 million. To put that in perspective, that’s more than the original 2012 Avengers film. It even cleared the per-episode cost of Game of Thrones Season 8, which sat at $15 million. Honestly, for a legal comedy, that budget is eye-watering.

In January 2024, star Tatiana Maslany hit the nail on the head during a Twitch stream: “I think we blew our budget, and Disney was like, ‘No thanks’” regarding a second season.

This is the central tension of the Marvel streaming era. A show can be a moderate hit with specific groups—She-Hulk crushed it with Gen Z, Black, and Hispanic households—but if it costs as much as a blockbuster movie, a “moderate hit” just doesn’t pay the bills.

Understanding the “Churn”#

Even with a massive reach, She-Hulk had a major retention problem. Leaked internal data from July 2024—the “Nullbulge” breach—showed that the series suffered from much higher churn than heavy hitters like Loki. Put simply: way too many people started the show but bailed before the finale. Honestly, it’s hard to blame them when the pacing felt that uneven.

When you pair high churn with a staggering production budget, your Return on Investment tanks. Marvel needs shows that keep subscribers hooked for the long haul. If your audience stops watching halfway through, that $225 million investment becomes a very expensive mistake.

The Legacy: A Shift in Strategy#

Was She-Hulk a success or a failure? Honestly, it’s both.

The show nailed it when it came to expanding the brand and grabbing the attention of younger, diverse audiences that Marvel was previously missing. But from a budget perspective? It was a disaster. Marvel isn’t quitting the TV game, but they’ve finally realized they need to rethink the math.

The “Marvel Spotlight” banner—which we saw with Echo—is a direct reaction to the She-Hulk fallout. They’re pivoting to smaller, lower-budget, standalone stories to stop bleeding money. They’re effectively admitting, “We love these characters, but we can’t drop $25 million per episode on every single one of them.”

It’s a smart move. Let’s be real, nobody needs every show to look like a Star Wars movie.

What’s the Lesson Here?#

If you’re a fan of the show, don’t let the numbers make you feel crazy. You are allowed to enjoy a project that didn’t turn into a decade-long, billion-dollar franchise. Honestly, sometimes it’s refreshing when a story just ends.

Here is how to look at the situation:

  1. Context is everything. When you see a headline screaming that a show is “a massive hit” or “a total disaster,” ignore it. The reality is always somewhere in the middle. Streaming services are currently struggling to balance the need for expensive “tentpole” blockbusters with the reality that they also need cheaper, niche content to fill out their libraries.
  2. Budget doesn’t equal quality. We assume more money means a better show, but She-Hulk proves that a bloated budget is often a death sentence. It’s hard to justify a renewal when you’re spending hundreds of millions of dollars on a niche comedy. Smaller, focused stories are usually better for everyone anyway.
  3. Diversity brought in new viewers. The data shows She-Hulk reached people outside of the traditional “hardcore” Marvel base. Even without a second season, that audience exists. They’re right there, and they’re loyal.

She-Hulk was a massive experiment. It tried to be a legal comedy, a superhero drama, and a meta-commentary on the MCU all at once. It failed to hit the “all-quadrant” success Marvel wanted for that $225 million price tag, but it did something more important: it provided a blueprint for how to recalibrate the Marvel machine.

Whether you loved the fourth-wall-breaking antics or thought the budget was a mistake, this is one of the most significant shows in the studio’s history. It’s not important because of how it ended; it’s important because of what it forced the studio to learn about the reality of streaming TV.
