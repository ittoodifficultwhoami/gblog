When the Side Characters Steal the Show: TV’s Best Pivots

Discover how iconic TV shows successfully shift focus from lackluster leads to compelling side characters, turning ensemble casts into the heart of the story.
April 13, 2026 · 6 min · MinWeb

  • When the “Star” Isn’t the Star
  • The Cost of the “Switch”
  • When the “B-Plot” Becomes the Goal
  • What This Teaches Us

Have you ever settled onto the couch, remote in hand, ready for the latest episode of a show you supposedly love, only to find yourself wishing the camera would just move on to literally anyone else?

It’s a jarring experience. You’re watching a series titled after a specific person, yet every time they appear on screen, the energy drops. You start cheering for the side characters, waiting for the B-plot to take over. Eventually, the show creators realize it, too. They quietly pivot, sidelining the “main” character to let the ensemble shine. Sometimes, they even pull off a full-blown narrative heist. Honestly, it’s usually for the best—I’d rather watch a compelling bartender than a brooding lead who has the personality of lukewarm tap water.

From space bounty hunters to political dramas, television history is littered with shows that discovered their leading man or woman was actually the least interesting person in the room. Here is why this happens and what it means for your favorite stories.

The “Trojan Horse” Strategy#

Sometimes, a creator knows exactly what they’re doing. They use a “Trojan Horse” strategy. They hook you with a conventional, relatable lead—usually white and middle-class—only to pull the rug out once you’re invested, forcing you into a much bigger, messier world.

Look at Orange Is the New Black. Jenji Kohan, the show’s creator, admitted in a 2013 interview that Piper Chapman was just a tool. She called Piper her “Trojan Horse,” a way to smuggle diverse, complex stories past executives and audiences who might otherwise ignore them. If you start with the girl next door, you can suddenly pivot to the lives of everyone else in the room. Honestly, it’s a brilliant cheat code for storytelling.

Sure, some fans eventually found Piper insufferable, but the show succeeded precisely because it stopped obsessing over her private drama. It shifted the spotlight to the systemic issues tearing apart everyone around her.

When the “Star” Isn’t the Star#

Sometimes, a casting shift isn’t calculated—it’s an emergency response to a creative epiphany.

In The West Wing, the original plan was for Rob Lowe’s character, Sam Seaborn, to be the narrative anchor. Meanwhile, Martin Sheen’s President Josiah Bartlet was only supposed to appear in four episodes per season. But once the writers saw the chemistry and sheer gravitas of Sheen’s performance, the show’s center of gravity shifted immediately. The creative team realized they had a masterpiece on their hands, and it simply wasn’t centered on the communications director. This lack of focus on Sam is exactly why Rob Lowe opted to leave the show in Season 4. Honestly, I don’t blame him—it’s hard to settle for a sidekick role when you were hired to be the star.

Legends of Tomorrow took a similar path. It started with Rip Hunter as the gritty, humorless protagonist. By the end of Season 1, audiences felt he was a bit “meh.” The producers actually listened. By the Season 2 finale, Rip stepped down as captain and Sara Lance took the helm. The result? A show that leaned into its chaotic, fun ensemble energy, becoming infinitely more beloved by its fanbase.

The Cost of the “Switch”#

Shifting the focus of a long-running show rarely goes smoothly. When a series replaces a beloved lead or suddenly pushes them to the sidelines, fans usually vote with their remotes. If the core anchor of a show walks, the fallout is never painless.

The departure of Steve Carell from The Office is the gold standard for this mess. His final episode, “Goodbye, Michael,” drew 8.4 million viewers, but the very next episode plummeted to 6.8 million. By the time the series wrapped, it had lost roughly 34% of its audience compared to the glory days of the Michael Scott era. (Honestly, it never really recovered.)

Then there is The Witcher. Losing Henry Cavill was catastrophic for the show’s metrics. Season 3 had already seen a 36% drop from Season 2, but the transition to Liam Hemsworth sent the numbers off a cliff. The Season 4 premiere saw a 51% decline from the previous season’s start, and audience scores on Rotten Tomatoes are stuck in the dismal 20% range.

When the “B-Plot” Becomes the Goal#

Sometimes a story shifts focus just to make the narrative sharper, even if the main character is still technically front and center. The Bear is a masterclass in this. While the show follows Carmy, it frequently pivots to dedicated side-character episodes that feel like self-contained short films. Episodes like “Honeydew,” which follows Marcus in Copenhagen, or “Forks,” centered on Richie’s transformation, build out the world in ways a singular focus on the protagonist never could. Honestly, I think those episodes are the best parts of the entire series.

The same goes for Andor. Even though the show is named after Cassian, it’s a sprawling political thriller with over 400 speaking parts. Creator Tony Gilroy didn’t mess around. Many viewers actually find the side players—like Stellan Skarsgård’s Luthen Rael or Andy Serkis’s Kino Loy—far more compelling than the man whose name is on the marquee.

What This Teaches Us#

So, what do these shows actually tell us?

  1. Titles are decoys. Don’t let a show’s name fool you. If the story feels stale, look at where the writers are actually dumping their energy. Often, the “main” character is just a bridge to a much more interesting ensemble.
  2. Growth requires shedding. Legends of Tomorrow thrived because they had the guts to retire a stagnant lead. Look at The Witcher for the flip side: pull out the heart of the show, and the whole structure collapses. It’s a painful lesson, but necessary.
  3. The ensemble is the goal. Great television is rarely about a single hero. Even in a show like Succession—which obsessed over Kendall Roy—the magic lived in that viciously talented ensemble. The Bear does this perfectly. It’s just more satisfying when everyone gets their moment to shine.

Next time you’re frustrated by an annoying lead, look at the background players. If you’re lucky, the showrunners are already planning to hand them the microphone.

And if you’re a writer: you don’t have to build the house around one pillar. It’s the rafters, the walls, and the foundation that actually hold the story together.
