Why Ukraine's Drone Makers Are Building Factories Abroad

If you’ve been following the news lately, you’re probably feeling a sense of whiplash. One day, you’re reading about major international defense firms partnering with Ukraine; the next, you’re seeing headlines about Ukrainian drone companies secretly building “parallel” factories abroad, bypassing their own government.

If you’re confused, join the club. The internet is awash with hot takes, ranging from accusations of corporate espionage to wild conspiracy theories about intellectual property theft. It’s easy to get lost in the noise, but the reality is a messy story about a nation under siege navigating the intersection of private innovation, survival-driven manufacturing, and the global arms market. Personally, I think it’s the most fascinating—and frustrating—aspect of the current conflict.

President Zelenskyy recently revealed that about 10 private Ukrainian drone factories are operating abroad “behind the state’s back.” To understand why, we have to look past the sensational headlines.

The Context: A War Driven by Rapid Innovation#

Ukraine has turned into a global laboratory for drone warfare. When the full-scale invasion hit in February 2022, the government banned all military exports. Overnight, the country’s tech startups had to pivot, morphing the local scene into a high-speed military industrial complex. Honestly, the speed of this transformation is terrifying.

We aren’t talking about basic surveillance quadcopters anymore. The focus has shifted to specialized interceptor drones—the “Sting,” the “Bullet,” and the “JEDI Shahed Hunter”—built to hunt down cruise missiles and Shahed loitering munitions. These are engineering marvels:

  • The “Sting” (Wild Hornets): Hits speeds of 315–343 km/h at altitudes up to 11 kilometers. All this costs between $2,100 and $2,500 per unit.
  • The “Bullet” (General Cherry): Reaches 310 km/h with 25 minutes of flight time.
  • The “JEDI Shahed Hunter”: Boasts a top speed of 350 km/h and handles altitudes up to 6,000 meters.

These drones rely on serious hardware, like the Kurbas-640a thermal camera and the $300 TFL Anti-Shahed AI module, which manages autonomous terminal guidance. It’s wild that they’re pulling this off with such cheap components.

Why the “Secret” Factories?#

When you hear that these firms are building facilities abroad, it’s not an act of betrayal. It’s a survival strategy.

For a private company in Ukraine, the domestic market is a minefield of red tape. The government enforces a 25% profit cap on state defense contracts. This rule stops war profiteering, sure, but it also chokes these businesses. They can’t scale, they can’t fund R&D, and they certainly can’t absorb the cost if a Russian missile levels their factory overnight.

By building satellite plants abroad, these firms are doing three things:

  1. Risk Mitigation: They are spreading their manufacturing footprint so that one strike doesn’t wipe out their entire production line.
  2. Access to Global Markets: The export ban is absolute. If these companies want to prove their tech works and generate the revenue needed to keep their Ukrainian operations afloat, they have to sell to international partners. Honestly, it’s the only way they stay in the game.
  3. Intellectual Property Protection: Ukraine is finally waking up to the importance of IP. The Ministry of Defense only set up an IP department in August 2024, and it took until April 2025 to pass a law restoring rights that were suspended when the war really hit its stride.

When a private company sells 1,000 interceptor drones to a foreign partner for $3.5 million while juggling a €300 million contract at home, they aren’t “stealing” from the state. They are spinning off their commercial arm to survive a war of attrition that is eating their domestic resources alive.

The “Double Game” of Global Partners#

This is where the frustration starts to boil over for industry observers. Major players like Baykar (Turkey) and Quantum Systems (Germany) are sinking deep roots into Ukraine, and they aren’t just here to visit.

  • Baykar: By late 2024, they dropped $100 million into three projects. Their factory near Kyiv was nearly 80% complete that October, with a goal of cranking out 120 drones every year.
  • Quantum Systems: They opened their first facility in April 2024 and are already prepping a second site for September 2025. They’ve committed €6 million to the region and are currently churning out 80 Vector drones a month.

Some critics claim these companies are just “scouting” the local tech, and honestly, they might be right to be cynical. Look at the March 2026 blowup: Rheinmetall’s CEO took a cheap shot at “kitchen-built” Ukrainian drones before being forced into a public apology. It perfectly captures the friction between legacy defense giants and the scrappy, fast-moving Ukrainian startup culture.

Why Does This Matter to You?#

The drone arms race isn’t just happening in government labs anymore. It’s moved to the front lines.

By pushing production inside Ukraine, companies are cutting down supply chains and making their tech harder to disrupt. It’s a bold move, honestly—building manufacturing hubs in a war zone shows just how vital these machines have become to modern strategy.

We’re seeing a shift toward decentralized, high-volume production. When private firms start building plants abroad just to bypass red tape, you know the demand for these systems is hitting a fever pitch. This isn’t just about military tech; it’s a complete overhaul of how we build and deploy hardware in a crisis.

Many readers fear that Ukraine’s hard-won technological secrets are being auctioned off to the highest bidder before the war is even over. There is a very real danger that “entrepreneurial sociopaths”—as one observer put it—will patent Ukrainian designs and then sue the very people who created them for royalties. Frankly, it’s a predatory move that reeks of wartime opportunism.

But we need to look at this through a more practical lens.

Ukraine isn’t going to win a war of attrition against a massive power purely on charity. They need a self-sustaining ecosystem. If these companies can create a “single window” for IP registration and leverage their global manufacturing bases to fund their work at home, they are building a blueprint for a modern, digital-native defense sector.

The bottom line#

This isn’t just about selling tech; it’s about survival. Building a functional industrial base under fire is messy and risky, but it is the only way to keep the innovation engine running when the headlines stop.

  1. Don’t mistake business for treason: Companies operating in a war zone face impossible financial choices. Opening a factory abroad is often just a way to keep the lights on back in Ukraine.

  2. Follow the IP, not just the hardware: The real value isn’t the physical drone. It’s the “Hornet Vision” AI, the sub-50ms guidance latency, and the thermal camera software. Keep an eye on how the MoD’s new Intellectual Property Department handles the “Single Window” patent platform. That’ll tell you more about the future of this war than any drone factory floor ever could—and honestly, that’s where the real power struggle is happening.

  3. Expect a “hybrid” industry: The future of defense isn’t just giant, slow-moving corporations. It’s a messy mix of legacy titans like Baykar and agile, internet-era startups operating across borders.

The “10 hidden factories” story isn’t a scandal. It’s a symptom of a country forced to innovate faster than global rules can handle. Ukraine is turning its survival into a competitive advantage, and the success of that experiment will define how nations defend themselves in the 21st century.
