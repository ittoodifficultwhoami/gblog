Buying a House After a Layoff: Should You Wait?

It’s the classic housing market gut punch: you stare at your rent statement, watch thousands of dollars vanish, and feel a sharp sting of regret. You tell yourself, “I’m just throwing money away when I could be building equity.”

Then reality sets in. Your income shifts. The market feels like a rollercoaster. Suddenly, you’re stuck at a crossroads, wondering if homeownership is a savvy financial move or a total trap.

If you’re making $90,000, have $95,000 in a High-Yield Savings Account, and are dealing with a recent layoff, the urge to quit renting is loud. But don’t rush into a 30-year commitment just yet. Let’s break down the math. There’s an old saying in finance that hits home for a reason: “Rent is the maximum you will pay for housing each month. A mortgage is the minimum.”

The “Single-Income” Hurdle#

The biggest hurdle is the bank itself. When you apply for a mortgage, lenders don’t care about your circumstances; they are cold, calculating machines obsessed with your Debt-to-Income (DTI) ratio.

If you apply solo, the bank ignores your spouse’s unemployment status entirely. They don’t count that income because, to them, unemployment benefits aren’t “stable or predictable.” You have to qualify for the loan based strictly on your $90,000 salary. Honestly, it’s a brutal way to look at a household budget.

Lenders use the “28/36 rule” to gatekeep. They want your housing costs—principal, interest, taxes, and insurance—under 28% of your gross income, with total debt payments capped at 36%. On a $90k salary, that puts your monthly housing budget at about $2,100.

Here is the problem: Realtor.com estimates show the median monthly mortgage payment sits closer to $2,675. Based on your single income, you won’t qualify for a typical home. Even if you found a way to squeeze in, you’d be stretched so thin that a single broken water heater would trigger a full-blown financial crisis.

Renting vs. Owning: The Hidden Math#

People love to say that paying rent is “throwing money away.” Honestly? That’s just a lazy take. Once you add up the real, unrecoverable costs of owning a home, the math tells a totally different story.

Buying a house isn’t just about the mortgage payment. You’re taking on a pile of extra expenses that simply don’t exist when you rent. According to Bankrate, the average “hidden” cost of homeownership—taxes, insurance, and the endless cycle of repairs—adds up to about $21,400 per year.

If you’re trying to figure out if buying actually makes sense, look at where that money goes:

Follow the “5% Rule” to clear the air: you should only buy if your annual rent costs more than 5% of a home’s total value. If your rent is cheaper than that, you’re better off renting and investing the leftover cash. Right now, renters save an average of over $900 a month compared to buying in 49 of the 50 major US metros.

The Cost of “Lock-in”#

Beyond your monthly cash flow, you have to think about geographic and financial flexibility. If a spouse loses their job, your family needs to be agile. You need the freedom to chase new opportunities or downsize in a heartbeat. Honestly, being able to pack up and leave without a lawyer and a mountain of paperwork is a superpower most people overlook.

Homeownership creates “geographic lock-in.” Selling a house is expensive. Between agent commissions, transfer taxes, and those annoying repairs needed to make a place “market-ready,” you will burn through 5% to 10% of the home’s value just to walk away. If you move within a few years of buying, you lose money. Period.

Look at the “breakeven horizon”—the time you must stay put for buying to beat renting. As of 2026, that sits at 5 years and 8 months. If you cannot commit to a house for nearly six years, keep your cash and your flexibility. You’ll be much better off.

The Opportunity Cost of Your $95k#

You have $95,000 sitting in a High-Yield Savings Account. It feels like a “down payment fund,” but that money is actually your emergency survival fund.

Financial advisors usually recommend keeping 6 to 12 months of expenses in cash for single-income households. If you dump that $95,000 into a house, you kill your liquidity. Honestly, it’s a terrifying position to be in. If your spouse is still looking for work or your income vanishes, you cannot “eat” your home equity.

Think about the opportunity cost, too. If that money were invested in a diversified stock portfolio, it would earn an average of 7–10% annually. By pulling that cash out to buy a house, you aren’t just spending the down payment; you are incinerating all the future compound growth that money would have provided.

Why the “Equity” Argument is Tricky#

Historically, homeowners have built massive wealth. Between 2000 and 2023, the average homeowner gained $376,722 in equity. Look at the data, and it’s clear: the median net worth of homeowners dwarfs that of renters.

But don’t let those stats fool you. This is a classic case of “survivorship bias.” Buying a house doesn’t automatically create wealth for everyone. Often, the people buying homes were already on a path to success—they had the stable income and savings to invest in other areas long before they signed a deed. Personally, I think we give houses way too much credit for “making” people rich.

If you jump into the market while financially stretched, you’re asking for trouble:

  1. Forfeiting Earnest Money: If your financing falls through—say, because of an unexpected job change—you lose that initial deposit. That’s a painful way to learn a lesson.
  2. Maintenance Debt: Homes aren’t static assets; they break. When the furnace dies or the roof gives out, you’re looking at thousands of dollars in repairs. If you have to put that on a credit card, you’ve just wiped out any “gain” you thought you were getting by avoiding rent.
  3. The “Minimum” Trap: Remember, rent is the maximum you pay each month. With a mortgage, your payment is just the starting line. When property taxes climb or insurance premiums spike, that bill goes up. Renters don’t have to worry about a surprise assessment or a crumbling foundation.

The Outlook for 2026#

It’s tempting to rush into buying because you’re afraid prices will only head north. Don’t panic. The forecast for the next two years is actually quite stable. Fannie Mae and the Mortgage Bankers Association expect interest rates to settle between 5.7% and 6.1% by the end of 2026. Meanwhile, J.P. Morgan predicts home price growth will stall at 0%. Honestly, waiting a bit doesn’t look like a bad strategy right now.

There is no “FOMO” here. The market isn’t signaling that prices will skyrocket in the next 18 months, which means you have time to breathe.

Practical Takeaways: What Should You Do?#

If your current rent feels like a burden, flip the script. You aren’t “throwing money away.” You are paying for housing security, flexibility, and a safety net while your family navigates a career transition. Honestly, keeping your options open is a massive win in this economy.

Follow this plan:

  • Prioritize Stability: Do not buy a home until your household income is rock solid. The stress of a mortgage is brutal when you’re relying on one income.
  • Protect the $95k: That money is your financial foundation. Keep it in a high-yield savings account or a similar liquid, low-risk spot. It’s earning interest while you wait for the housing market to settle and your job situation to stabilize.
  • Check Your 5-Year Plan: If you can’t say with 90% certainty that you’ll be in the same city and job for the next five years, keep renting. Renting is a smart financial strategy that protects your capital and keeps your life flexible.
  • The “Wait and See” Approach: With home price growth stalling and interest rates easing, the market will be more favorable to buyers in 2027 than it is today. Use this time to stack more cash, focus on your career, and build your confidence.

Buying a house is a massive financial commitment. There is no shame in choosing the stability of a rental contract while you get your family’s finances back to a place of strength. Take a breath, keep your cash safe, and wait until the math—and your life—truly align in your favor.
