Found an Old Microsoft Stock Certificate? How to Verify It

Finding a physical stock certificate in your late father’s belongings feels like finding a buried treasure map. You’re holding a piece of history, but a wave of questions hits you immediately: Is this still worth anything? Did he sell it years ago? Does this paper hold value in the digital age, or is it just a sentimental memento?

Finance is intimidating, especially when you’re staring at legal documents from decades ago. But you aren’t alone. Thousands of families find physical certificates tucked into old filing cabinets or safe deposit boxes every year. Tracking this down is a solvable problem if you know who to call and what to ask. (Honestly, I’ve seen people find certificates for companies that don’t even exist anymore, so keep your expectations grounded.)

Here is how to decode that certificate and see if you’re holding a golden ticket.

First, the Reality Check: It’s Not Just One Share#

If your father bought a single share of Microsoft in 1991, you need to understand the “magic” of stock splits. Microsoft has grown massive since then. To keep their stock price from becoming too expensive for regular people, they’ve chopped their shares up into smaller pieces over the years.

Since late 1991, Microsoft has split its stock seven times:

  • June 27, 1991: 3-for-2 split
  • June 15, 1992: 3-for-2 split
  • May 23, 1994: 2-for-1 split
  • Dec 9, 1996: 2-for-1 split
  • Feb 23, 1998: 2-for-1 split
  • March 29, 1999: 2-for-1 split
  • Feb 18, 2003: 2-for-1 split

Those seven events are why a single share from 1991 turned into 72 shares today. Honestly, it’s wild to think about how much that adds up.

With Microsoft trading at about $424.16 per share in April 2026, that one original share is worth roughly $30,539.52. That’s just the raw share value. If your father had a Dividend Reinvestment Plan (DRIP) set up—which basically just uses your payouts to buy more stock automatically—the total value sits somewhere between $120,000 and $150,000. It pays to be patient.

Your First Point of Contact: Computershare#

You don’t need to call Microsoft’s corporate headquarters to track down your stock. Like most major companies, Microsoft hires a “Transfer Agent” to keep the master list of shareholders.

For Microsoft, that company is Computershare. They hold the official ledger. They will tell you exactly whether your certificate is still active, if it was already converted to electronic shares, or if the money was sent to the state as unclaimed property. (Honestly, dealing with these agencies is a headache, but they’re your only path forward.)

How to get in touch:

  • Official Web Portal: www.computershare.com/microsoft
  • Customer Service (U.S.): 1-800-285-7772 (Select Option 1)
  • Customer Service (International): +1-425-706-4400

The Verification Process#

When you call Computershare, they’ll need specific details to look up your father’s account. Keep the certificate in front of you and be ready to rattle off these three things:

  1. The Certificate Number: You’ll find this printed right on the face of the document.
  2. Registered Name: Give them the exact name as it appears on the certificate. If it’s a joint account, have both names ready.
  3. Tax Information: The Social Security Number or Tax ID of the person the stock was registered to.

Since this certificate belonged to your late father, Computershare requires legal proof of your authority to handle his affairs. You’ll need to provide a certified copy of his Death Certificate and Letters Testamentary—that’s the court document naming you as the executor. Dealing with probate paperwork is a headache, but you’ll need these originals to move forward.

What if the Certificate is “Missing” or “Inactive”?#

Sometimes, a certificate sits in a frame, but the shares have been “escheated.” This happens when a stock account goes dormant for three to five years. If a company can’t reach the owner to send dividends or annual reports, they are legally required to hand those assets over to the state of the owner’s last known residence.

If Computershare tells you they no longer hold your shares, don’t panic. The money isn’t gone; it’s just waiting for you at a state unclaimed property office. Honestly, it’s a lot more common than people realize.

  • Search Nationally: Start at MissingMoney.com. It’s the best database for tracking down unclaimed property across almost every state.
  • Check Washington State: Since Microsoft is based in Washington, a huge amount of unclaimed property ends up there. Check ClaimYourCash.org to be safe.
  • The “Money Match” Program: In places like Washington, if the property is under $1,000, the state might just mail a check to the rightful heir automatically. No paperwork required.

If you lost the physical certificate, the company will force you to purchase an “Indemnity Bond.” This is basically an insurance policy that protects the company in case that old certificate ever pops up again. It usually costs 2% to 3% of the current stock value, which is a total pain, but it’s the price of admission for getting your money back.

Checklist for Your Records#

TaskAction Needed
Verify StatusCall Computershare with your Certificate number and SSN.
Legal ProofKeep your Death Certificate and Letters Testamentary handy. You’ll need them immediately.
Search StatesUse MissingMoney.com to hunt for any escheatment. This site is surprisingly reliable.
TransferAsk for a Medallion Signature Guarantee if you’re selling or moving the assets. Don’t skip this; the banks are sticklers about it.
AnalyzeCompare the current value ($30k+) against the original investment.

Do This Today#

Stop waiting. First, grab that Certificate number and call Computershare to see if the account is still active. If you’re handling an estate, organize your legal documents into a single folder right now. Trust me, digging for a Death Certificate while on hold with a transfer agent is a nightmare you want to avoid. Once the paperwork is sorted, run a search on MissingMoney.com to make sure the state hasn’t already snatched the funds. Finally, pull the numbers and decide if it’s actually worth moving the money or if you should just leave it where it is.

Feeling overwhelmed? Take a breath and handle this one step at a time. Here is your roadmap:

  1. Don’t sell yet. Before doing anything with the certificate, confirm you have the legal right to move the assets. If you’re unsure about the Letters Testamentary, talk to an estate attorney.
  2. Get a Medallion Signature Guarantee. If you eventually sell the stock or move it into a brokerage account, you will need this. It’s not just a standard notary stamp; it’s a specific financial guarantee. You can usually get this at your local bank branch. Honestly, they don’t always make this part easy, so be prepared for a bit of back-and-forth.
  3. Ask the right question. When you call Computershare, skip the small talk and ask: “Is this certificate still valid, or were these shares escheated to the state?” That one question saves you weeks of being bounced between the wrong departments.
  4. Relax. You’re dealing with assets that have sat untouched for over 30 years. There is no fire to put out today. Take your time and get your documents in order.

Finding this certificate is a cool way to connect with your father’s financial history. Whether it turns out to be wall art or a significant inheritance, you’re doing the necessary work to close out his estate. Treat it with care, stick to the official channels, and keep a paper trail of every conversation you have with the transfer agent.
