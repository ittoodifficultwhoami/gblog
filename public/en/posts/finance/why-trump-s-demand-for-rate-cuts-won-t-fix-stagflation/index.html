Why Trump’s Demand for Rate Cuts Won’t Fix Stagflation

March 20, 2026 · 4 min · MinWeb

  • Why Interest Rates Cannot Fix This

The President wants lower interest rates, but the Federal Reserve is going to ignore him. Despite the shouting matches in the news, the White House has trapped itself. They are pushing for lower rates to save the economy, but their own geopolitical decisions are the very reason the economy is stalling.

We are stuck in a Stagflation Trap. Prices are high, jobs are shaky, and while it is easy to blame the people at the Fed, they are currently helpless against the forces actually driving your costs up.

The Reality of the Current Standoff#

On March 18, 2026, the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) voted 11–1 to keep interest rates steady between 3.5% and 3.75%. You might hear the President calling Fed Chair Jerome Powell names like “Too Late,” but the Fed is playing a losing game.

You cannot fix a supply shortage by printing more money.

When the administration launched Operation Epic Fury in late February, it triggered a mess that is hitting your wallet harder than any central bank policy ever could. The conflict closed the Strait of Hormuz. That single move cut off 20% of the world’s liquefied natural gas and pushed Brent crude oil to $126 per barrel.

When energy gets expensive, everything follows suit. This is a supply-side shock. If the Fed cut rates right now, they would be pouring monetary gasoline on a fire that is already consuming the economy.

Why Interest Rates Cannot Fix This#

Think of the economy like a car. The Federal Reserve manages the speed by using the gas or the brake. Right now, the car is overheating because it has no fuel.

If you press the gas pedal while the tank is empty, you do not go faster. You just destroy the engine. Here is the reality of our current situation:

  • Energy Prices: Brent crude hit $126 a barrel after the conflict started. When transport and manufacturing costs jump, businesses pass those bills directly to you.
  • Fertilizer Shortages: Urea prices are up 40% to $600 per tonne. This is not just a high gas price issue; it is a direct threat to food production costs for the coming year.
  • Job Market Cooling: We lost 92,000 nonfarm payroll jobs in February 2026. Unemployment is up to 4.4%. This is why the President is demanding cuts, but the Fed is locked on the 3.1% core inflation rate, which is still too far from their 2.0% target.

The Political Theater#

Ignore the social media spats. There is a serious constitutional fight happening behind the scenes.

The administration nominated Kevin Warsh to replace Powell, but that process is dead in the water. Meanwhile, the Department of Justice is digging into the Fed’s $2.5 billion headquarters renovation project with a criminal probe.

U.S. District Judge James Boasberg recently blocked the DOJ subpoenas, pointing out that the investigation looks like a pretext for harassment. Even the President’s own party is pushing back. Senator Thom Tillis is actively blocking the transition process, calling these moves a power grab that threatens the independence of the central bank.

What Does This Mean for You?#

If you want to know what happens next, look at the calendar. Jerome Powell stays in an acting capacity if a successor is not confirmed by mid-May. Expect this stalemate to last all spring.

Here is the practical takeaway for your planning:

Do not count on cheaper borrowing costs anytime soon. The Fed is focused on fighting inflation caused by the energy crisis, not saving the labor market. As long as energy and fertilizer prices remain elevated, the high-interest-rate environment stays.

Watch the Strait of Hormuz. This is the single most important indicator for your finances right now. If the conflict in the Middle East de-escalates and energy prices drop, the Fed gets room to cut rates. If the conflict continues, prepare for higher prices and higher interest rates.

Ignore the political noise. Politicians love to blame the Federal Reserve because it is a punching bag that cannot fight back. The reality is that the economy is being driven by broken supply chains. Making financial decisions based on the promise of immediate rate cuts is a bad idea when the underlying costs of living are still rising.

The best strategy is to assume rates will stay high for a long time. Keep your debt manageable and prepare for the ripple effects of the energy crisis, such as higher grocery bills and slower business growth. The headlines are loud, but the global energy market tells the only story that matters.
