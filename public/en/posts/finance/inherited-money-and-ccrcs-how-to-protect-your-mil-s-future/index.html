Inherited Money and CCRCs: How to Protect Your MIL’s Future

Protect your mother-in-law’s CCRC residency and Medicaid eligibility by learning how to properly manage an inheritance without triggering contract penalties.

It feels like winning the lottery—a sudden, unexpected inheritance arriving right as your mother-in-law moves into a Continuing Care Retirement Community (CCRC). But in the world of high-stakes senior living, “luck” comes with a complex set of legal and financial strings.

If you are holding a check right now, your first instinct is probably to pass it to the grandkids or tuck it into a savings account. Stop. In the world of long-term care, one well-intentioned decision today leads to an eviction notice or a denied Medicaid application tomorrow. And trust me, fighting the government on benefits is nobody’s idea of a fun weekend.

Navigating the intersection of CCRC contracts, Medicaid eligibility, and probate law requires total precision. Here is how to handle that windfall without sabotaging your MIL’s future.

The CCRC Contract: Your First Line of Defense#

Most people assume that because it’s her money, she can do whatever she wants with it. When you live in a Continuing Care Retirement Community (CCRC), that’s rarely true.

Industry data from 2024–2025 shows the average entrance fee for these places lands between $300,000 and $400,000, with luxury units hitting $1,000,000+. Since these communities are designed to provide lifetime care, they have a huge stake in your mother-in-law’s financial longevity. They don’t want her running out of cash.

  • Asset Dissipation Clauses: Many CCRC contracts explicitly ban “gifting” assets if it prevents the resident from covering their monthly bills. The median monthly fee for independent living reached $4,253 in early 2025, and most facilities raised their rates by 5% to 6% last year to cover rising labor costs. They keep a close eye on your accounts for a reason.
  • The “Benevolent Care” Trap: If she receives any kind of subsidy or “benevolent care” from the facility, she is contractually obligated to report any change in her financial status, including an inheritance. Hiding that cash is a breach of contract, plain and simple.
  • Refundable Contracts: About 80% of entrance-fee CCRCs offer contracts where 50% to 90% of the fee returns to the estate. If she dies, the facility is already on the hook to pay that back. Adding an inheritance to her estate complicates the probate process, so talk to the facility’s financial office before shifting any money around.

The Medicaid Minefield: Why “Gifting” Backfires#

If your mother-in-law relies on Medicaid—or expects to down the road—you need to understand the 60-Month Look-Back Period.

People often assume they can just “disclaim” or refuse an inheritance to keep it off the books. It’s a common mistake, and it’s a dangerous one. The law is brutally simple here: even if a “Qualified Disclaimer” (IRS Section 2518) works for your taxes, Medicaid treats it as a direct transfer of assets.

The government sees you walking away from that money as a deliberate attempt to look “impoverished” so you can qualify for taxpayer-funded care. They aren’t going to just let that slide. When this happens, they trigger a penalty period calculated using the state’s Medicaid Penalty Divisor. Honestly, it’s a trap most people don’t see coming until it’s too late.

Critical Rules to Remember#

  1. Reporting is mandatory. If your MIL is on Medicaid, that inheritance counts as income the month she gets it and an asset every month after. You usually have just 10 days to report it to the Medicaid office. Honestly, do not miss that deadline—they are ruthless about it.
  2. “Good intentions” don’t count. A 2025 Texas ruling confirmed that even talking to an executor about distributing an inheritance is treated as “accepting” the money. Once you cross that line, you cannot disclaim it later to fix a Medicaid error.
  3. Know your state’s rules. Most states cap assets at $2,000, but California axed the asset test for Medi-Cal entirely last year. Still, stay sharp. Laws change every year, like North Carolina’s new HB 357, which forces more transparency for retirement communities.

Protecting the Inheritance: Legal Strategies#

If you want to manage the money without disqualifying her, you have three primary paths. Do not try these on your own—get an elder law attorney involved.

  • Exempt Assets: Not every purchase is a “gift.” You can use the inheritance to pay for things that don’t count against Medicaid limits, such as essential home repairs, one vehicle, or an irrevocable funeral contract.
  • Pooled Income Trusts: For anyone over 65, a pooled trust holds the inheritance in a managed account. The funds pay for “supplemental needs”—like specialized equipment or travel—that Medicaid won’t touch.
  • Special Needs Trusts (SNT): If your MIL is disabled, a first-party SNT holds the inheritance, allowing her to benefit from the cash while keeping her government eligibility intact.

Practical Takeaways: What You Should Do Right Now#

  • Secure the documents. Gather the inheritance paperwork immediately.
  • Call an attorney before depositing a dime. If the money hits her personal bank account first, the clock starts ticking and the headache begins.
  • Report the change. Contact the caseworker within the 10-day window to avoid penalties.

If you just discovered an inheritance, take a breath. Don’t act on impulse. Follow these four steps instead:

  1. Freeze the Funds: Park the money in a neutral, interest-bearing account. Do not spend, gift, or move a single cent. Any transaction creates a paper trail you’ll eventually have to explain to a caseworker, and trust me, that’s a headache you don’t want.

  2. Read the CCRC Contract (Twice): Find the sections on “Financial Eligibility” or “Asset Disclosure.” See exactly what the facility requires you to report. You should be prepared to disclose information in writing, but only after you’ve reviewed the specific requirements with a professional.

  3. Hire an Elder Law Specialist: Do not rely on a general practice attorney or your regular tax accountant. You need an expert who lives and breathes the intersection of state-specific Medicaid rules, the 60-month look-back, and CCRC contracts. Ask them point-blank: “If we keep this money, how does it affect the residency agreement? If we put this in a trust, does the state treat it as a transfer?”

  4. Stay Proactive: New legislation—like Maryland’s mandate for resident involvement or Washington’s SB 5691—means government oversight is at an all-time high. The days of “hiding” assets or hoping nobody notices are over.

An inheritance should be a comfort, not a crisis. By taking a slow, methodical approach and prioritizing legal compliance over emotional, quick-fix decisions, you ensure your mother-in-law’s long-term care plans remain secure and fully funded.
