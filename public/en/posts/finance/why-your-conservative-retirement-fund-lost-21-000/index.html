Why Your Conservative Retirement Fund Lost $21,000

March 27, 2026 · 5 min · MinWeb

Imagine opening your retirement statement expecting your savings to have grown over the last decade, only to find that $60,000 has shrunk to $39,000. It’s a punch to the gut. You expect a “conservative” fund to be a safe harbor—a place where your money works quietly and reliably, protected from the wild swings of the stock market.

If you are looking at a statement like this, your frustration is backed by the math. Seeing a 35% loss over 12 years in a fund designed for stability is not a standard market outcome. It’s a signal that something deeper—likely a combination of high costs, specific market events, and complex structural fees—has been eating away at that balance.

To understand how this happened, we have to look past the “conservative” label and peel back the layers of how these investments actually function.

The Illusion of the “Conservative” Label#

The John Hancock Multimanager Lifestyle Conservative Portfolio (JALRX/Class A) is primarily invested in fixed-income assets, typically holding about 80% in bonds and 20% in equities.

In a normal market, this is a balanced approach meant to dampen volatility. However, 2022 was anything but normal. It was the worst year in modern history for the bond market, with the Bloomberg U.S. Aggregate Bond Index losing 13%. Because JALRX was heavily tilted toward bonds, it took a massive hit, dropping roughly 17.3% that year.

But one bad year doesn’t explain a 12-year decline. To understand the $21,000 loss, we have to look at the “hidden” drag on your mother’s account.

Death by a Thousand Cuts: Fees and Expenses#

When you invest in mutual funds, you aren’t just paying for the performance of the stocks and bonds inside. You are paying a team of managers, marketing departments, and administrative platforms. In many 401(k) or brokerage environments, these fees stack up in ways that are nearly invisible on a monthly statement.

If your mother was in a plan where these fees hit the industry average—often between 1.50% and 1.80%—she was fighting an uphill battle from day one. When you combine a high-fee structure with a year like 2022, the account doesn’t just stagnate; it actively bleeds value.

The Reality Check: Share Price vs. Total Return#

There is a common point of confusion when looking at long-term statements: the difference between share price and total return.

As of early 2026, the share price (NAV) of the JALRX fund sits between $12.27 and $12.40. If you look back at 2014, that same share price was roughly $12.80 to $13.10. On paper, the share price has barely moved.

However, mutual funds pay dividends. If an investor reinvests those dividends, a hypothetical $10,000 investment made in 2014 would have grown to approximately $15,500 to $16,000 by early 2026. While that’s a positive return, it’s modest compared to the broader market. If your mother’s account is worth less than the initial $60,000, it suggests one of three things:

  1. High Fees: The total costs were higher than the dividends and interest earned.
  2. Withdrawals: Sometimes, what looks like a “loss” is explained by automated withdrawals, Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs), or hardship withdrawals that the account owner may have forgotten.
  3. Class Choice: If she was placed in Class C shares (JCLCX) rather than Class A, the annual expense ratios are higher, which compounds the impact of market downturns.

A Pattern of Regulatory Issues#

Beyond the mechanics of the fund, John Hancock has faced a series of legal and regulatory hurdles. These don’t necessarily explain the specific performance of your mother’s fund, but they provide context for why the customer experience feels so disjointed:

  • 2021 ERISA Settlement ($14M): A case involving allegations of self-dealing and high fees within the firm’s own employee retirement plans.
  • 2022 SEC Settlement ($21.3M): A fine for failing to disclose revenue-sharing payments.
  • 2022 New York Settlement ($21.6M): Related to the handling and termination of long-term care policies.
  • 2025/2026 Legal Activity: Ongoing lawsuits regarding data breaches and life insurance policies suggest a company currently struggling with significant administrative pressure.

In 2026, many customers have reported issues ranging from tax form errors to significant delays in accessing funds. If the account balance seems wrong, a clerical error in tax processing or a failure to properly credit dividends could be a factor.

What Should You Do Now?#

If you are looking at a $21,000 loss, it is time to move from “watching” to “investigating.” Here is your roadmap:

1. Demand a Detailed Statement of Cash Flows Do not rely on the “current value” summary. Ask the firm for a document showing every single transaction—contributions, dividends reinvested, management fees, and, crucially, any withdrawals—from 2014 to the present. You need to see if money left the account through withdrawals you didn’t know about. Honestly, some of these statements are intentionally confusing.

2. Audit the Fees Check the plan documents to see the specific share class and the total expense ratio. If the all-in cost is exceeding 1.5% annually, you are paying the firm to hold your money while it loses value.

3. Check for Administrative Errors Given the recent reports of 1099-R errors and tax processing issues, ensure that all dividends have been properly reinvested and that the account hasn’t been subject to any “ghost” fees.

4. Rethink the Strategy If this fund doesn’t match your mother’s needs, or if the fees are too high, it may be time to seek an independent financial advisor—someone who does not benefit from the “wrap” fees or commissions associated with these products.

You are right to be skeptical. A conservative portfolio should not feel like a shrinking asset. While the bond market crash of 2022 explains some of the pain, it does not account for a 35% decline over 12 years. Dig into the statement, look for hidden fees, and verify that there haven’t been unauthorized or forgotten withdrawals. Your mother’s retirement savings deserve a much closer look.
