Post-Mastectomy Pain Syndrome: Why You Aren't Alone

If you are suffering from chronic pain after a mastectomy, you aren't alone; learn the truth about Post-Mastectomy Pain Syndrome and how to reclaim your life.
April 6, 2026 · 5 min · MinWeb

You survived one of the most frightening diagnoses a person can face. You underwent a mastectomy to remove cancer, did exactly what the doctors ordered, and expected a quick recovery. But instead of healing, you are left with a terrifying new reality: constant, stabbing, electric-shock pain. Simple things—wearing a shirt, sitting in a chair, or hugging a loved one—now feel like torture.

If this sounds familiar, or if you are staring at a medical chart feeling completely unheard, you aren’t alone. And more importantly, you aren’t crazy.

An investigative report from KFF Health News and USA Today on April 2, 2026, exposed a hard truth that patients rarely hear during pre-surgical consultations: Post-Mastectomy Pain Syndrome (PMPS). Surgeons are often so focused on cutting out the cancer that they gloss over the reality of severed nerves. It’s a massive blind spot in the medical community, and frankly, it’s infuriating.

Here is why this happens, why it gets ignored, and how you can actually fight back.

The Reality of the “Invisible” Pain#

PMPS is not just “residual soreness” from an incision. It is a chronic neuropathic pain condition recognized by the International Association for the Study of Pain.

According to investigative reporting by Brett Kelman, this syndrome affects between 25% and 60% of women who undergo mastectomies. To put that in perspective: if you are in a support group with ten other women who have had this surgery, there is a very high probability that several of you are suffering in silence.

The pain is often harrowing:

  • “Electric shocks” radiating through the chest and back.
  • “Stabbing” sensations beneath the armpit.
  • “A tight band” constricting the chest, making every breath feel restricted.
  • Allodynia: A condition where even the lightest touch—like clothing rubbing against the skin or a pet brushing against your chest—triggers agonizing pain.

For patients like Sophia Bassan, who was 43 when she experienced these symptoms, the impact went beyond the physical. She couldn’t wear a shirt or even lift a fork due to the severity of the shocks. The disability was so profound that she lost over $200,000 in wages. Some patients are forced to sleep sitting up for months because lying flat is simply too painful.

Why Does This Happen?#

To understand the pain, you have to look at the anatomy of the surgery. Nerves are inevitably cut.

Dr. Jonathan Bank, a plastic surgeon and founder of the Breast Relief™ clinic, identifies the root of the problem: the pain is triggered by nerves that are severed and left “raw.” When a nerve is cut and left to fend for itself, it becomes hypersensitive, creating a “short circuit” that sends chronic pain signals to the brain. It’s a mechanical failure of the body’s wiring, honestly.

Research shows that certain surgical and treatment factors increase the odds of developing this syndrome:

  • Axillary Lymph Node Dissection (ALND): This increases the risk of chronic pain by approximately 2.04 times.
  • Postoperative Radiotherapy: Radiation adds a layer of damage, with an odds ratio of 1.35.
  • Age: Younger patients are at a higher risk. The danger increases for every 10-year decrement in age, specifically for patients 49 and younger.

The Gap in Care: Why Surgeons Don’t Warn Patients#

One of the most frustrating aspects of this issue is that surgeons rarely warn patients about permanent nerve pain during the “informed consent” process.

There are two main reasons for this. First, many surgeons view removing the tumor as the only metric for success. Second—and more critically—most breast surgeons are not trained in the microsurgical techniques required for nerve repair. If a surgeon doesn’t know how to fix a nerve, they aren’t going to talk about the risk of damaging one.

This leaves a massive hole in the healthcare system. Patients are often told, “The surgery went well, the pain should go away soon,” even when the pain is clearly neuropathic and permanent.

Hope on the Horizon: Nerve Reconstruction#

The narrative is starting to change, thanks to advocates like Terri Coutee of the DiepCjourney Foundation and innovators like Dr. Bank.

For years, doctors treated nerve pain with pills or a “wait-and-see” approach that rarely worked. Today, there are surgical interventions that address the “raw” nerves directly.

Nerve reconstruction involves suturing severed nerves or using grafts to restore sensation and stop the “short-circuiting.” Another promising technique is Neurectomy with Dermal Sensory Peripheral Nerve Interfaces (DSPNI). A 2025 study showed this procedure could reduce average pain scores from 6.9 down to 0.6.

What Should You Do?#

If you are struggling with pain after a mastectomy, take these steps to advocate for yourself:

  1. Validate Your Experience: Your pain is real and documented by the IASP. If your care team tells you it’s “all in your head” or “normal,” get a second opinion.
  2. Look for Specialized Care: Not all plastic surgeons are trained in nerve reconstruction. Search for clinics that focus on “neurotization” or “post-mastectomy nerve pain.” Look for terms like “multidisciplinary approach.”
  3. Prepare for Consultations: If you are pre-surgical, ask your surgeon directly: “What are the risks of nerve damage, and what is your plan to prevent or address chronic neuropathic pain if it occurs?” Their answer tells you everything about their expertise.
  4. Find a Community: Organizations like the DiepCjourney Foundation provide vital resources. Connecting with others who understand the “tight band” feeling helps.
  5. Don’t Give Up: Medicine is fallible, and there is a long delay between research and clinical practice. However, the rise of specialized clinics shows that the medical community is finally waking up.

Your surgery was meant to give you a future. You deserve to live that future without being trapped in a cycle of avoidable pain. You are your own best advocate—keep asking questions, keep looking for answers, and never settle for being told that your pain doesn’t matter.
