Is the 'Wait Until Ready' Potty Training Method Failing?

It’s a scene playing out in classrooms everywhere: the bell rings, kids file into their desks, and teachers try to start their lesson plans. But before they can teach reading or math, they spend an average of 2.5 hours a day dealing with “school readiness” issues. In plain English? They’re changing diapers and cleaning up accidents for four- and five-year-olds.

If you feel like potty training has become a moving target, you aren’t imagining things. There is a quiet, messy crisis unfolding in our schools. For decades, we’ve been told that “child-led” training is the gold standard. But the data from 1957 to 2025 shows a startling trend: we’ve gone from nearly universal training by 18 months to a point where one in four children starting primary school in the UK still isn’t potty trained. Frankly, that’s a disaster for everyone involved—especially the teachers.

Our modern, “wait-until-they-are-ready” philosophy has backfired, and it’s setting our children back.

The Great Potty Shift: How We Got Here#

To understand why this is happening, we have to look at the history of the diaper.

Back in 1957, a staggering 92% of U.S. children were fully potty trained by the time they turned 18 months old. If you suggest that to a parent today, they’ll probably look at you like you’re insane. So, what changed?

In 1962, Dr. T. Berry Brazelton published his “child-oriented” approach in Pediatrics. He told parents to wait for clear signals from the child before starting the process. Right around that same time, a convenient new invention hit the market: disposable diapers. As these products took over the shelves in the 70s and 80s, the “wait-until-ready” approach became the gospel. Predictably, the average age for potty training began to climb.

Here is the kicker: Dr. Brazelton became a paid consultant for Procter & Gamble, the makers of Pampers, shortly after his theories took off. It’s hard to ignore the profit motive there. As one report from UCL’s The Big Toilet Project recently pointed out, “Waiting for readiness” is an outdated concept in an era of ultra-absorbent disposables.

The Numbers Tell a Different Story#

The data from the last few years is sobering. Look at the numbers:

  • 2024–2025: 25% of children in the UK start primary school still in diapers or pull-ups.
  • 7%: Kids aged 4 or 5 who remain in diapers full-time.
  • 61%: Kids in 2025 who are fully potty trained by 30 months.

Compare that to 2004, when 95% of children were trained by that same age, and it’s clear: we aren’t just shifting the timeline. We’re slowing it down significantly, which—let’s be honest—is a massive headache for every teacher and parent involved.

The “Sweet Spot” vs. The Myth of Waiting#

Every parent worries about starting potty training too early. Rushing a child before they are ready causes real problems; in fact, research shows that training before age two triples the risk of daytime wetting and chronic constipation.

But the “wait until they’re ready” philosophy isn’t foolproof either. If you wait too long, you’ll actually face more resistance, not less. Trust me, fighting a toddler who has developed a strong preference for diapers is an uphill battle you don’t want to pick.

Here is how the timeline actually shakes out:

Age RangeAverage Time to Complete Training
18–24 months13–14 months
27 months and older10 months or less

The “sweet spot” sits between 27 and 32 months. By this age, children have the cognitive development to grasp the process and the physical maturity to control it. You’ll get a faster, much less stressful transition.

Why “Waiting” Can Backfire#

Many parents hold off on potty training to dodge the inevitable mess, but the “wait-and-see” approach often backfires. If you push training until a child is three or four, they’ve usually developed a stubborn attachment to their diapers. Honestly, breaking that habit feels like trying to convince a toddler that broccoli is candy—it’s a battle you don’t need.

Waiting for “readiness” also ignores the reality of chronic constipation. This is the primary medical reason potty training fails for kids aged 3–5. When children withhold stool because they haven’t mastered the process, their rectum stretches. Eventually, they stop feeling the urge to go altogether. This leads to encopresis—a fancy term for involuntary stool leakage. It’s a stressful, painful condition, and frankly, it’s a nightmare for everyone involved.

Is It Time to Rethink Our Strategy?#

If you’re currently stuck in a battle with a toddler who refuses to use the potty, take a breath. You aren’t a bad parent, and your kid isn’t “broken.” You are simply trapped in an outdated model that prioritizes their comfort over their independence.

Let’s face it: cleaning up accidents is exhausting, but holding on to diapers is only making the problem last longer.

1. Ditch the “Wait Forever” Mentality#

Readiness doesn’t just magically happen one day. It’s a skill built through repetition and exposure. You don’t need to force your child, but you should create an environment where using the toilet is the standard expectation—not an optional lifestyle choice. Honestly, I think kids pick up on that shift in attitude faster than we give them credit for.

2. Try the “Underwear Trick”#

If your kid honestly doesn’t care that their pull-up is soaking wet, try putting cotton underwear underneath the pull-up. It’s a simple trick that forces them to feel that gross, damp sensation against their skin while keeping your floors and sofa safe from puddles. Honestly, it’s the best way to bridge the gap between the comfort of a diaper and the reality of wearing underwear.

3. Try Structured Training#

The Azrin & Foxx Method is all about “scheduled training.” Forget the shaming and the pressure—this is just a clear, no-nonsense framework. It works, too. Studies show a 93% success rate for kids over 25 months. Frankly, I think toddlers secretly love the routine; it takes the guesswork out of that constant “do I need to go?” anxiety.

4. Separate Day from Night#

Don’t let bedwetting—or nocturnal enuresis, if you want to get clinical—stall your progress with daytime potty training. It’s actually super common; one in five five-year-olds still wets the bed.

Daytime control and nighttime control are two completely different physiological processes. Your child can be a total pro at using the toilet all day long while still needing a pull-up at night. Honestly, don’t sweat the nighttime stuff yet. Just focus on the day and give their body the time it needs to catch up while they sleep.

The American Academy of Pediatrics puts it clearly: “There is no evidence that toilet training is harmful to a child’s development when it is done in a gentle, non-coercive manner.”

We’ve become so afraid of “pushing” our kids that we’ve essentially stopped teaching them. By waiting for them to take the lead, we’re waiting for a signal they often don’t know how to give.

If you’re feeling the pressure, remember: you’re the parent. You can introduce the potty, provide the training, and set the expectation. Your child is capable of more than we give them credit for. Honestly, your future self—and your laundry budget—will thank you for it.
