Why You Should Prepare a "Parting Bag" for Your Pet

Prepare for your pet's final transition with dignity by creating a parting bag, ensuring you can focus on honoring their memory instead of logistical stress.

It is a truth universally acknowledged—or at least felt by 97% of pet owners—that our pets are family. Yet, despite that bond, society rarely prepares us for the logistics of saying goodbye.

We spend years planning for our pets’ lives. We curate their nutrition, schedule vet checkups, and manage their pain with modern treatments like monoclonal antibody injections. But when the unthinkable happens, we are left scrambling in a state of emotional paralysis.

If you have felt frustrated, overwhelmed, or blindsided during a pet’s final decline, you are not alone. There is a growing movement toward proactive “end-of-life planning.” One of the most compassionate ways to honor your pet and spare yourself unnecessary trauma is to assemble a “parting bag” well before the inevitable day.

Why You Need a “Parting Bag”#

A “parting bag” is a prepared kit designed to help you handle the immediate aftermath of a pet’s passing with dignity.

Physical changes occur rapidly after death. Rigor mortis typically begins between 10 minutes and 3 hours, and fluid release is a normal part of the process. Having a dedicated, leak-proof, and biodegradable bag—like those from Euthabag—allows you to keep your pet comfortable and clean. It buys you time to coordinate transport or home-burial arrangements without the urgency of a frantic medical situation.

If you cannot arrange for immediate aftercare, keep the body cool or refrigerated if it won’t be transported within 4–6 hours. Having these supplies on hand ensures you aren’t searching for gear while you’re grieving. Honestly, nobody wants to be shopping for plastic sheeting while they’re crying over their cat.

The “Before and After” Mindset#

One of the most valuable lessons from pet owners who have walked this path is simple: Don’t wait until the final hour to capture memories.

Many owners feel pressured to decide on mementos like paw prints at the moment of passing. However, the emotional toll of that moment, combined with the physical discomfort an aging pet feels, makes that timing difficult.

Gather these items while your pet is still comfortable:

  • Paw Prints: Air-dry clay takes 24–48 hours to harden, and salt dough requires a bake. Doing this during a “good day” avoids the stress of doing it when they are in pain.
  • Nose Prints: A sterling silver impression is a timeless keepsake, but do this while the pet is still mobile.
  • Fur Clippings: Keep a small vial or a locket ready.
  • DNA Storage: The pet DNA testing market is exploding. You can now store genetic data for thousands of years.

The Move Toward Home-Based Care#

In the past, we felt obligated to take our pets to the clinic for their final moments. Today, 53% of veterinary clinics offer or facilitate at-home euthanasia. The trend is clear: most families now prefer a home setting.

At-home services are popular because they allow the pet to be in their favorite spot, surrounded by familiar scents, reducing the anxiety of a car ride. While travel fees add $50 to $300 to the total cost, the “best possible” experience rating suggests it is an investment in peace of mind.

Defining “Quality of Life”#

How do you know when it is time to put together that parting bag? The transition from “senior” to “declining” is often subtle.

Vets often recommend the HHHHHMM Quality of Life Scale to monitor their status:

  • Hurt
  • Hunger
  • Hydration
  • Hygiene
  • Happiness
  • Mobility
  • More Good Days

Also, consider the “Red Lines” strategy. Pick three things your pet absolutely loves—like greeting you at the door or eating a specific treat. If they lose the ability to perform two of those, their quality of life has diminished. If your pet has Canine Cognitive Dysfunction, watch for “sundowning,” or increased nighttime restlessness. Tracking these symptoms provides the clarity needed to make the tough decisions.

Your Action Plan#

You don’t have to do everything at once, but take these small steps today to secure your future peace of mind:

  1. Start a “Memory Folder”: Gather favorite photos and store them in a digital memorialization service.
  2. Purchase the “Parting Bag”: Keep a professional-grade, leak-proof, biodegradable bag in a safe place. Include absorbent puppy pads. You hope never to use it, but having it is a form of love.
  3. Research Local Providers: Look up mobile euthanasia services and aquamation providers in your area. Bookmark their websites.
  4. Create Keepsakes Early: Plan a quiet afternoon to make a paw print or a fur clipping. Do it when your pet is relaxed.
  5. Have the “What If” Conversation: Talk to your family about their wishes. Does everyone want to be present? Do you want a burial or a cremation?

Saying goodbye is the hardest part of being a pet parent. By moving from avoidance to preparation, you aren’t signaling that the end is near—you are ensuring that whenever that time comes, you are free to focus entirely on love.
