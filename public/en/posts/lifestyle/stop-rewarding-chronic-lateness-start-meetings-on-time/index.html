Stop Rewarding Chronic Lateness: Start Meetings on Time

March 26, 2026 · 5 min · MinWeb

We’ve all been there. You’re sitting in a conference room—or staring at a blank Zoom screen—waiting for “that one person.” You know the one. They aren’t just five minutes behind; they are consistently, reliably, and frustratingly late.

While you sit there tapping your pen, you think you’re being polite. You aren’t. You’re fueling a cycle of dysfunction.

Chronic lateness isn’t just a minor annoyance; it’s an economic drain and a massive hit to team morale. As of 2024, the estimated annual cost of employee lateness to the U.S. economy is a staggering $61 billion. When you break that down to the individual level, a worker who loses just 35 minutes per week to lateness costs their employer an average of $166 annually. For “chronic offenders” who show up 10 minutes late every single day, that cost jumps to $500–$600 per year.

If you are tired of being the person who waits, stop rewarding the behavior. Your “politeness” is making the problem worse, and it is time to reclaim your time.

Why We Wait (And Why It Fails)#

The desire to wait for latecomers usually comes from a place of kindness. We don’t want people to feel left out. However, by delaying the start of a meeting, you are telling the latecomer that their time is more valuable than everyone else’s.

Psychologically, this is a reward for lateness. When you recap everything they missed the moment they walk in, you teach them that there is no consequence for tardiness. In one documented case, a group moved a meeting from 10:00 AM to 10:30 AM specifically to accommodate a chronic latecomer. The result? The latecomer didn’t show up until 10:45 AM. By pushing the start time, the organizers simply pushed the finish time back. Honestly, people will expand to fill the time you give them.

Data confirms this: 50% of virtual meetings start late, with an average delay of 75 seconds. That sounds small, but it sets a cultural tone that the start time is just a suggestion.

The Biology and Psychology of the Clock#

Not everyone who is late is being malicious. There are deep-seated reasons why some people struggle with time:

  • Time Blindness: Popularized by Dr. Russell Barkley in 2001, this refers to a genuine deficit in perceiving the passage of time, often linked to dopamine dysregulation. It is common in individuals with ADHD.
  • The Planning Fallacy: Humans are notorious optimists. We underestimate how long a task will take by 40%.
  • Personality Differences: Even personality type plays a role. Type A and Type B personalities don’t experience a minute the same way. Research shows a 19-second discrepancy in how these groups perceive the passage of one minute. A Type A person feels a minute has passed at 58 seconds; a Type B person might not feel it until 77 seconds have ticked by.

Regardless of the “why,” the impact is undeniable. Retail environments see a 2.3% drop in sales for every 1% increase in lateness. When the supply chain is involved, the impact is even more severe; in 2025, late deliveries accounted for an average 8% revenue loss for companies.

The Solution: The “Hard Start” Rule#

The most effective way to address chronic lateness is to implement a “Hard Start” policy. The meeting begins at the exact second it is scheduled, regardless of who is in the room.

Refuse the Recap When the latecomer arrives, do not pause the meeting. Do not provide a summary of what they missed. If they ask, “Can you get me up to speed?” the answer is simple: “We are mid-discussion; you can catch up after or check the notes.” By refusing to recap, you remove the reward for being late.

Use the “Choir Effect” If you’ve ever been to a rehearsal, the music starts whether you are in your seat or not. You quickly learn that 7:00 means 7:00. Apply this to your workplace: if you are facilitating, start the content at the scheduled time.

Implement the 50-Minute Hour If your team is constantly late because they are moving from one meeting to another, stop scheduling 60-minute blocks. Switch to 50-minute meetings. This provides a ten-minute “transition buffer” for restroom breaks, water, or simply walking to the next room.

Separate Your Transportation If your partner or a friend is the source of your lateness, take separate vehicles. Don’t let someone else’s lack of planning become your emergency.

Stop Feeling Guilty#

Setting boundaries isn’t mean; it’s professional. If you are struggling with a parent, partner, or colleague who is always late, use the “Mother’s Strategy”: Set a firm departure time and leave.

If you tell a friend, “I am leaving for the restaurant at 6:00 PM,” and they aren’t there, you leave at 6:00 PM. By doing this, you shift the responsibility of punctuality back to where it belongs: on the individual. If they miss the movie, that is a natural consequence of their time management, not a failure of your hospitality.

Your Action Plan#

  • For the Leader: Start meetings on time, every time. Do not stop to bring late arrivals up to speed.
  • For the Punctual Victim: Stop waiting. If you’re meeting for lunch, arrive on time, order your food, and start eating.
  • For the Chronically Late: You are falling for the “Planning Fallacy.” Start tracking your time. Most people rely on simple lists rather than actual time management. Use time blocking, set alarms for when you need to start getting ready, and be honest about your transit times.

Lateness is a habit that thrives on the leniency of others. By refusing to accommodate it, you aren’t just saving yourself frustration—you are doing the latecomer a favor by finally letting them experience the natural consequences of the clock. Stop waiting. Start doing.
