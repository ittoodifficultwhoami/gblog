Don't Quit Your Job Before Using Your Vacation Days

You’ve slogged through the year. You hit your deadlines, kept your cool, and banked every hour of vacation time on your balance sheet. Then, you decide it’s time to move on. You hand your manager your resignation letter and suddenly hit a wall: What happens to those three weeks of vacation I never took?

This frustration hits millions of workers every year. We tend to assume that since we “earned” those days, they belong to us just like money in a savings account. In reality, employment law is a mess—and more often than not, it’s a total loss for the employee.

If you don’t know the rules before you put in your notice, you’re essentially leaving thousands of dollars on the table. Honestly, it’s a gut punch. Here is the reality check you need, and why your best strategy is always to burn through those vacation days before you walk out the door.

The “Use It or Lose It” Reality Check#

The biggest trap for American workers is the assumption that vacation time is a universal right. It isn’t.

Federal law does not require employers to pay out your unused vacation time when you leave a job. Under the Fair Labor Standards Act, the Department of Labor kicks the responsibility down to state laws and your company’s internal policies. That’s a massive gap in protection.

If you live in a state without specific laws on your side and your employee handbook says “use it or lose it,” you are walking away from actual money. You’ve earned that time; treat it like a paycheck. American workers lose about $65.5 billion in benefits every year by letting those days evaporate. The total financial liability for unused PTO in the U.S. has hit over $1 trillion in 2024, which averages out to roughly $7,600 per full-time worker.

Honestly, leaving that kind of cash on the table is just handing a bonus back to your boss.

Not All States Are Created Equal#

Since there’s no federal mandate on paid time off, your rights depend entirely on your zip code. Some states view vacation time as a vested wage—money you’ve already earned that no one can snatch away. Others see it as a discretionary perk your boss can pull at any time. It’s a frustrating mess, honestly, but here is how it shakes out.

State Law Breakdown#

  • The “Protective” States: In places like California, Colorado, Illinois, Massachusetts, Montana, and Nebraska, earned vacation time is treated as a wage. Once you’ve banked those hours, they are yours. If you leave your job, your employer must pay them out.
    • In California, “use-it-or-lose-it” policies are flat-out illegal.
    • In Colorado, a 2021 Supreme Court ruling confirmed that vacation pay cannot be forfeited for any reason, even if you’re fired for cause.
  • The “Policy-Driven” States: In states like Texas, Florida, and Georgia, there’s no law mandating a payout. Your rights here are dictated entirely by your employment contract or the company handbook. If your handbook says they don’t pay out, they don’t have to. Check the fine print, because they’ll definitely use it against you if they can.
  • The “Conditional” States: New York generally requires a payout, unless the employer has a written policy explicitly stating that vacation time is forfeited when you leave—and they have to make sure you actually saw that policy in writing.

What About “Unlimited” PTO?#

“Unlimited” PTO has turned the payout conversation into a total mess. Since these policies don’t technically track hours—there’s no “bank” of days to pull from—most companies argue there’s nothing to pay out when you quit. Honestly, it’s a clever loophole for them, but a frustrating one for us.

If you’re on an unlimited plan, don’t count on a check for your unused time. Just be careful: in states with tough labor laws like California or Colorado, the courts are getting smarter. If a company enforces “implied caps” on their unlimited policy, the law treats that time as accrued wages anyway. They owe you that money.

The Global Take#

If you’re reading this from outside the United States, your situation is probably much clearer. The rules are straightforward.

  • Canada: Every province and territory legally requires employers to pay out earned but unused vacation time when you leave a job. Accrual rates usually start at 4% of your gross wages (two weeks) and bump up to 6% (three weeks) after 3 to 15 years, depending on where you live. Saskatchewan is the outlier here—they require a 3-week minimum starting on your very first day. Honestly, the rest of the country should take notes.
  • United Kingdom: You have a statutory right to be paid for any holiday you’ve accrued but haven’t taken yet. This covers at least 5.6 weeks per year, and the reason you’re leaving doesn’t matter. If you’re an hourly worker, employers calculate this using a 52-week average “holiday pay reference period.”

The “Unlimited” Trap#

Companies love to brand “Unlimited PTO” as a shiny perk, but it’s often a clever accounting trick. If you aren’t accruing specific days off, the company has no balance to pay out when you quit. Personally, I’d take three weeks of guaranteed vacation over “unlimited” days any day of the week. If you’re in a state that treats PTO as wages, this policy effectively lets your employer skip that payout obligation entirely. Check your handbook before you get too excited.

About 60% of U.S. companies pay out unused PTO when you leave, but that leaves a massive 40% chunk of employers who just pocket those hours. It’s a total loss for the employee.

Around a quarter of companies also offer “cash-out” programs, letting you trade unused time for a paycheck while you’re still on the clock. If you have access to one of these, take it. Don’t wait until you’re quitting to hope for a payout; that’s when things get messy and you’re most likely to walk away with nothing. Honestly, I’d rather have the extra cash in my bank account now than gamble it on a company policy that might change by the time I hand in my two weeks.

Practical Takeaways: What Should You Do?#

If you’re planning to quit, don’t leave money on the table. Before you hand in that notice, run through this checklist to make sure you get what you’re owed.

  1. Read your employee handbook. This is your primary source of truth. Look for words like “payout,” “separation,” and “accrued leave.” Does the company pay out unused days, or do they vanish the moment you walk out the door? You need to know now, not after your last day.
  2. Know your state laws. If you’re in the U.S., check your state’s Department of Labor website. If your state mandates payouts, you have leverage. If it doesn’t, you’re at the mercy of your contract—and frankly, most companies won’t go out of their way to be generous.
  3. Use the time first. This is the golden rule: just take the vacation. If you have two weeks banked, schedule that time off before you resign. It is much easier to enjoy a paid break while you’re still on the payroll than it is to fight HR for a check after you’ve already turned in your badge.
  4. Don’t assume “unlimited” means cash. If you have an unlimited PTO policy, you get $0 for your unused time. If you have “accrued” time, assume you get nothing unless you see a specific policy or state law that forces them to pay up.
  5. Check your pay stubs. If you are in a region where a payout is mandatory, audit your final paycheck. Employers sometimes “forget” or miscalculate the value of those final days.

Your vacation time is part of your compensation—you earned it. Don’t let it slip away. If you have the days, use them. It’s the simplest way to make sure your exit is as profitable as it is peaceful.
