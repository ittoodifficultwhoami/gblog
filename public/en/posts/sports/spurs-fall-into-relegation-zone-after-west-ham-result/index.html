Spurs Fall Into Relegation Zone After West Ham Result

If you support Tottenham Hotspur, the last few months have felt like watching a slow-motion car crash. You can’t look away, even though you really want to. For years, the talk around the club was all about how close they were to finally grabbing a trophy. Now? The conversation has shifted to a much darker question: Are they actually going to get relegated?

As of April 11, 2026, the unthinkable is a statistical reality. West Ham United dismantled Wolverhampton Wanderers 4-0 on April 10, leapfrogging Tottenham in the table. That result pushed Spurs into 18th place. They are officially in the drop zone, which is frankly humiliating for a club of this size.

For most supporters, this isn’t just a bad run of form. It’s a historic crisis. Here is the data behind the collapse and what it means for the final stretch of the season.

The Numbers Behind the Nightmare#

The numbers don’t lie: this club is in a freefall. Looking at the raw data, it’s obvious why the fanbase is losing its mind right now.

  • League Position: 18th.
  • Points: 30 points from 31 matches.
  • The Winless Streak: Tottenham hasn’t won a Premier League game since New Year’s Day. That’s 13 straight matches without a win (5 draws, 8 losses). Frankly, it’s embarrassing.
  • The Goal Drought: The team hasn’t scored in their last four home matches. That’s a new club record for pure futility.

To put this disaster in perspective, you have to go way back. This is the first time Tottenham has been in the bottom three since January 2009. You have to look all the way back to the 1976/77 season—the year they were relegated—to find the last time they were this deep in the mud this late in the year.

The bottom of the table is a dogfight, and Spurs are sitting right in the danger zone. As of April 11, 2026, here is the state of play:

A Season of Upheaval#

Don’t forget, just one year ago, the club was hoisting the UEFA Europa League trophy, finally ending that 17-year drought. Since then, the whole organization has hit an identity crisis.

Selling Son Heung-min to LAFC in August for $26.5 million left a massive hole in the attack that nobody has managed to plug. Off the pitch, Daniel Levy resigned as chairman in September. That ended an era, sure, but it also left a leadership vacuum that the club is still failing to fill.

Then there’s the managerial carousel. It’s been dizzying:

  • Thomas Frank: Sacked February 11.
  • Igor Tudor: Sacked March 29, after just 44 days.
  • Roberto De Zerbi: Hired March 31, with a massive five-year contract.

De Zerbi has a mountain to climb. His first game is Sunday, April 12, away at Sunderland. He’s taking over a team that has forgotten how to win, let alone find the back of the net.

Can They Stay Up?#

The experts are split, but they’re mostly leaning toward the same grim reality: a drop is looking likely.

Back in early April, the Opta Supercomputer pegged the risk of relegation at 27%. Since then, the bookies have gotten even more pessimistic, pushing the odds anywhere from 6/4 to 5/2. That puts the true probability of going down between 28% and 40%. (Honestly, I wouldn’t want to be the one checking those betting slips right now.)

These numbers aren’t a death sentence, but they are a wake-up call. Spurs are in a proper dogfight, and at this point, they have zero margin for error.

The “Run-In” to Remember#

The biggest concern for Spurs fans isn’t just the current form; it’s the nightmare schedule waiting for them. If they want to stay in the Premier League, they have to survive this absolute gauntlet:

  1. Sunderland (Away) – A massive “six-pointer” to kick off the De Zerbi era.
  2. Brighton (Home) – Their one real shot to end that pathetic home goal drought.
  3. Wolverhampton Wanderers (Away) – A scrap against a team clawing their way out of the mud.
  4. Aston Villa (Away) – A miserable trip to play a side fighting for Europe.
  5. Leeds United (Home) – This is the one. It’s where their fate gets decided.
  6. Chelsea (Away) – A London derby where nobody hands out favors.
  7. Everton (Home) – The season finale. Frankly, this is going to be the most stressful 90 minutes in modern Tottenham history.

The 1976/77 Warning#

If you’re a fan of club history, the 1976/77 season is the ultimate cautionary tale. Tottenham finished 22nd that year with just 33 points, letting in a brutal 72 goals—the worst defensive record in the First Division at the time. They were relegated, plain and simple. Still, they proved that a collapse isn’t the end of the road.

They bounced back to the top flight immediately in 1978 after finishing 3rd in the Second Division. They even put up a historic 9-0 drubbing of Bristol Rovers along the way. That era is best remembered for “The Great Stitch Up,” where they dragged themselves to promotion on the final day with a nerve-wracking 0-0 draw against Southampton. Honestly, I don’t know how the fans didn’t have heart attacks watching that. Modern supporters would definitely prefer a less stressful path to safety, but it’s a good reminder: even after the deepest falls, a club can rise again.

The Takeaway#

Focus on the long game. You don’t need a perfect season to build a winning culture—you just need a club that knows how to grit its teeth and climb back out of the hole.

If you’re feeling frustrated, anxious, or just plain tired of the discourse, you’re not alone. Here is how to handle the next few weeks:

  • Manage Expectations: Stop assuming a “big club” is immune to relegation. The league table doesn’t care about history or prestige—it only cares about points on the board.
  • Watch the “Six-Pointers”: Forget the big headlines. Focus entirely on games against teams around you like Sunderland, Wolves, and Leeds. These matches mathematically determine if Spurs stay up.
  • Lower the Pressure on the New Manager: Roberto De Zerbi has barely had time to meet the squad. Expecting an immediate, tactical revolution is a pipe dream. Give the players time to actually learn his system before you start calling for his head.
  • Avoid the “Panic Narrative”: It is way too easy to get sucked into the online doom-mongering. The situation is objectively bad, but there are enough games left to scrape together the points needed for survival.

At the end of the day, football is about the highs and lows. We’re in a massive low right now, and the final few weeks are going to be agonizing. Whether you’re watching in silence or shouting at the TV with family, remember that the season isn’t over until the final whistle on the last match day. For now, all eyes are on the Stadium of Light this Sunday.
