Does the thought of cooking dinner just make you tired? You aren’t the only one. Many of us grew up thinking that cooking requires strict recipes, surgical precision, and a final plate that tastes like a five-star restaurant.

But what if you didn’t have to be perfect? Yoshiharu Doi, one of Japan’s most respected culinary authorities, suggests that for a daily meal, “it doesn’t have to be delicious.”

He isn’t advocating for bad food. It’s a liberating philosophy that changed the lives of thousands. Take the author Gislomac, for example; he went from never cooking at all to making miso soup every single day for a year. Honestly, if he can manage that, the rest of us have no excuse. Here is why this simple approach actually works.

The Philosophy of “Ichiju Issai”#

In 2016, Yoshiharu Doi published a book called Ichiju Issai de Yoi to iu Teian—or, A Proposal That One Soup and One Side is Enough. The concept is refreshingly simple: you don’t need a complicated spread. A bowl of miso soup, a bowl of rice, and you’re set. Honestly, looking at my own kitchen, I’m usually just making a mess anyway.

By September 2022, the series had sold over 380,000 copies. People are clearly starving for this back-to-basics approach. The core of his teaching is blunt: “It’s okay to be messy. Just boil what you have.”

When Gislomac first tried this, they noted: “The taste is thin, but it feels like I’m eating something a human made.” That realization is the secret to why this habit actually sticks. Cooking isn’t an engineering project meant for Instagram; it’s an act of humanity.

Why “Not Delicious” is Actually a Good Thing#

When Doi says, “It doesn’t have to be delicious,” he isn’t telling you to eat garbage. He’s drawing a line between “gourmet” food—the stuff meant to show off—and the food you actually eat to survive the week.

Daily food needs to be a flavor you don’t get tired of. Gourmet meals are great for a special occasion, but they’re usually too heavy or intense to handle every single night. Let’s be honest: nobody wants a five-course meal on a Tuesday. By letting go of the pressure to cook a culinary masterpiece, you lower the stakes. You stop obsessing over seasonings and just focus on the simple, necessary act of feeding yourself.

The 4-Step Method#

Forget the elaborate prep work. If you want to eat like this every day, keep it simple.

  1. Clear the fridge. Stop shopping for missing ingredients. Use what you already have, even if it looks like a random assortment.
  2. Focus on the boil. You don’t need a fancy technique. Just get your water hot and let your ingredients soften. It’s honest cooking.
  3. Prioritize comfort. Aim for food that feels good, not food that looks good on Instagram. If it warms you up, you’re doing it right.
  4. Lower the bar. Perfection is the enemy of a consistent habit. If it’s edible and nutritious, consider it a win. Seriously, stop stressing over the plating.

You don’t need any kitchen experience to get started today. Gislomac’s take on the Doi method is refreshingly minimalist:

  1. Water: Pour one bowl of water into a pot.
  2. Add Ingredients: Toss in whatever is lurking in your fridge. Use vegetables, mushrooms, or even leftover bacon or chicken. Throw in 2–3 dried sardines (niboshi) if you want real depth. Honestly, the sardines are a game changer.
  3. Boil: Cook until everything is tender.
  4. Miso: Turn off the heat before stirring in the miso. This keeps the flavor bright.

This is the beauty of “Miso Universality.” Miso is incredibly forgiving. If you get bored, you don’t need a fancy new recipe—you just need to swap in different “taste changers.”

Easy “Taste Changers” for Your Miso Soup#

Ways to Level Up Your Dish#

  • Creaminess: Stir in butter, soy milk, or a splash of heavy cream.
  • Zest: Add ginger juice, fresh grated ginger, or lemon salt.
  • Umami: Mix in Parmesan, tomato puree, or shio koji. This stuff is a total flavor hack.
  • Heartiness: Toss in Spam, sausage, or a soft-boiled egg.

A Quick Reality Check#

Don’t go overboard. If you throw every ingredient on this list into one bowl, you’ll ruin the balance. Pick one or two boosters and stop there. Your taste buds will thank you.

Context is everything. There’s a funny ongoing debate in the Japanese online community about this. Some people took the “don’t have to be delicious” advice so literally that they started serving meals that were genuinely unappetizing. Their families eventually started joking, “I wish he hadn’t said that.” Honestly, I can’t blame them—nobody wants to eat a sad bowl of mush.

The takeaway is simple: Doi’s advice is for self-nourishment. If you are cooking for others, keep the balance and flavor in mind, but drop the guilt. You don’t need to be a Michelin-starred chef to put a warm, healthy, decent bowl of soup on the table.

Why This Matters in 2026#

This philosophy is blowing up right now. With the 2024 release of the Jounetsu Tairiku documentary and the upcoming English translation of Ichiju Issai in 2025, the world is clearly pivoting toward this sustainable, low-pressure way of living. Honestly, it’s about time.

In our frantic culture, standing in your kitchen for ten minutes to boil water and some vegetables is a genuine act of self-care. It’s a chance to pull out that wooden bowl you never use, find a moment of actual stillness, and remind yourself that you’re fully capable of taking care of your own needs.

What Should You Do Now?#

You don’t need a pile of expensive ingredients or a three-hour cooking tutorial to make something good.

Here is your action plan:

  1. Check your fridge: Find a vegetable or leftover that’s sitting there looking lonely.
  2. Boil it: Toss it in a pot with some fish stock. If you don’t have stock, plain water works just fine (honestly, I do this all the time).
  3. Add Miso: That’s it.
  4. Practice: Do this for a few days. You aren’t aiming for a Michelin star; you’re aiming for the quiet satisfaction of making something with your own hands that actually sustains you.

Cooking isn’t a chore. It’s a simple, human rhythm in an otherwise noisy life. Start tonight—the pot is waiting.
