Living in Shimbashi: A Tokyo Doctor’s Local Dining Guide

Ever wondered what it’s like to live, work, and eat in the exact same building? Most of us waste hours commuting, battling train delays and dreaming of cutting the distance between our desks and our beds. For one doctor in Tokyo, that’s not a fantasy. It’s reality. He spends over 97% of his life inside a single structure.

Meet Shosuke Takeuchi, a doctor and member of the YouTube group “Tokyo Hiyarihatto.” He lives on the upper residential floors of the legendary New Shimbashi Building. His “100% eating out” lifestyle has become a full-blown urban legend, and honestly, I can see the appeal. In his Tokyo Hiyarihatto Report No. 32, he pulls back the curtain on what it’s like to live as a hermit-scholar in the middle of Tokyo’s busiest district.

The Man Who Never Leaves Shimbashi#

Dr. Takeuchi isn’t just a local doctor; he’s a neighborhood institution. As the director of the Takeuchi Internal Medicine Shimbashi Ekimae Clinic, he keeps a grueling schedule: 9:00 AM to 9:00 PM, 365 days a year. They call it “Kuji-kuji.” When you spend 12 hours a day seeing patients and live just a few floors above your office, you inevitably become a human encyclopedia of the local dining scene. Honestly, I’m not sure how he doesn’t go stir-crazy.

For Dr. Takeuchi, the world ends at the borders of the Shimbashi district. To put his extreme focus into perspective, he considers “Nan-oh Park” in Nishi-Shimbashi local, but “Minami Sakurai Park” in Saitama? That’s strictly out of range. He isn’t kidding. His lifestyle is as hyper-focused as a city-state like the Vatican.

The New Shimbashi Building: A Vertical City#

If you’ve ever walked past the New Shimbashi Building, you know it’s a chaotic maze of discount ticket shops, massage parlors, and hidden eateries. Covering about 58,107 m² across 15 floors, it’s a genuine labyrinth. You will get lost, and honestly, that’s half the fun.

The building operates in distinct zones. The lower floors are a hive of activity, especially the first floor, where 9 to 11 major discount ticket shops are crammed into a tiny footprint. Head up to the 10th and 11th floors, though, and the vibe shifts entirely. This is the “Takeuchi House,” a residential and office space designed so the doctor can navigate his entire life without ever stepping out into the Tokyo humidity or rain. Smart guy.

A Curated Guide: Where the Doctor Actually Eats#

Dr. Takeuchi eats every single meal out, so his recommendations are genuinely battle-tested. He skips the tourist traps for the spots that actually fuel his grueling “Kuji-kuji” work schedule.

The Changing Landscape of Shimbashi#

The New Shimbashi Building isn’t going to be around forever. The entire area around Shimbashi Station is being gutted and rebuilt. By 2037, this part of Tokyo’s skyline will be unrecognizable.

The current plan? Replace the existing footprint with at least two high-rise towers, both pushing 30 stories and standing over 120 meters tall. As of February 2025, the redevelopment association for the East Exit—where the sister Shimbashi Ekimae Building sits—is officially up and running. Honestly, it’s a shame to see such a unique, neon-soaked piece of history get the wrecking ball. If you want to see these retro monoliths in the flesh, don’t wait too long. Your window is closing.

Takeaways for the Curious Urbanite#

You don’t have to live in a single building 97% of your life to learn something from Dr. Takeuchi. Here are three ways to sharpen your own routine:

  1. Become a “Local Expert”: Stop chasing the “top 10” restaurants across the entire city. Instead, hunt for the five best spots within a five-minute walk of your desk. Developing a genuine relationship with a few vendors makes the daily grind feel like a community rather than a commute. Honestly, there’s nothing better than a shop owner knowing your usual order before you even open your mouth.

  2. Simplify Your Logistics: If you hate your commute, shrink your radius. Find a gym near your office or pick up groceries on the same route every day. These small changes cut down on decision fatigue—and trust me, you have enough of that already.

  3. Appreciate the “Retro”: Massive redevelopment projects are coming for Shimbashi. If you want to experience the Showa-era charm of the New Shimbashi Building, go now. Places like Beefun Azuma and Ogawaken are more than just restaurants; they are living snapshots of Tokyo history that won’t look the same in a decade.

Dr. Takeuchi’s life is eccentric, sure, but it hits on a beautiful truth: in a city as overwhelming as Tokyo, finding a small corner to call your own is the ultimate luxury. Whether you’re stopping for a bowl of tantanmen or wandering through the ticket shops, take a moment to look up at those upper floors. You’re likely looking at the home of the man who never leaves his favorite neighborhood.
