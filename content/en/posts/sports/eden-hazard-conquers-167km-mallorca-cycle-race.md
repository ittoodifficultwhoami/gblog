Have you ever finished a grueling workout—lungs burning, legs like lead—and thought, "I’ve earned this," only to reach for a greasy burger and a beer instead of a kale smoothie? You aren't alone. In fact, you’re in the same mental headspace as one of the most talented footballers of his generation: Eden Hazard.

Watching professional athletes retire is usually a somber affair. We’re used to seeing them trade the spotlight for a quiet life of punditry or coaching. Eden Hazard, who hung up his boots in October 2023, took a different approach. Instead of fading away, he’s hitting the pavement—literally.

On April 26, 2025, Hazard tackled the Mallorca 312, a legendary amateur cycling Gran Fondo in Spain. If you think this was a casual Sunday cruise, think again. He conquered 167 kilometers (roughly 103 miles) of punishing terrain, and the internet couldn’t get enough of it.

## The Brutal Reality of 167 Kilometers

The Mallorca 312 isn’t a stroll through the park. It’s a massive endurance event capped at 8,000 riders, designed to test the limits of even the most dedicated enthusiasts. Hazard’s route spanned 167 km through the Serra de Tramuntana mountains. 

To put the scale of this challenge into perspective:

| Metric | Hazard’s Stats |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Total Distance** | 167 km (103 miles) |
| **Finish Time** | 7 hours, 30 minutes, 30 seconds |
| **Elevation Gain** | ~2,475 meters (8,120 feet) |
| **Average Speed** | 22.2 km/h |

Moving from 90 minutes on a football pitch to a 7.5-hour cycling slog is a massive shift in aerobic output. The elevation gain alone is staggering—climbing over 8,000 feet is like scaling a proper mountain range. By the end, even Hazard had to admit, "The last 25 kilometers were impossible."

## The "Burgergate" Jersey: A Masterclass in Self-Deprecation

One of the funniest details from the event—and one that set the internet on fire—was the jersey Hazard wore. While most cyclists don expensive, aerodynamic kits, Hazard wore a custom white and red jersey featuring a giant logo for "Quick," a Belgian fast-food chain.

This wasn't random. It was a hilarious, self-aware nod to "Burgergate"—a 2011 incident where Hazard was spotted eating a burger after being subbed off during a match for the Belgian national team. 

In a world where elite athletes are usually hyper-curated and PR-managed, seeing a superstar lean into his own meme is refreshing. It’s the ultimate "I know what you’re thinking, and I’m in on the joke" move. Honestly? We need more of that.

## The "Recovery" Protocol: Beer and Brutal Honesty

If you’ve ever felt guilty for having a post-workout snack that wasn't "on-plan," Hazard’s post-race behavior is the validation you’ve been waiting for. Upon crossing the finish line in Playa de Muro, there was no protein shake or ice bath. He just grabbed a beer.

It’s the kind of humanizing moment that makes people love an athlete all over again. In his documentary series, *The Hazards: Hors Catégorie 2*, he spoke candidly about the toll the ride took. He told reporters in Spanish, *"Tengo que entrenar un poco más y perder un poco de peso y entonces será perfecto"* (I have to train a little more and lose a bit of weight, and then it will be perfect).

This honesty is a huge shift from the secrecy of professional football. He isn't worried about his market value anymore. He’s just a guy who realized that 167 kilometers is really, really hard, and he’s willing to admit he isn't at his peak.

## Why This Matters

Why does the world care that a retired footballer rode a bike in Mallorca?

1.  **The "Relatable" Elite:** Watching Hazard tackle the same struggles as any amateur cyclist—the burning legs, the need to shed a few pounds—reminds us that these giants are still human. 
2.  **The Retirement Pivot:** Many of us struggle with finding a new identity after a major career change. Seeing Hazard trade the high-pressure world of professional football for personal athletic challenges shows that retirement doesn't have to be the end of movement; it can be the start of something new.
3.  **Community Connection:** Despite being a global superstar, he rode alongside his father, Thierry Hazard, and thousands of other amateurs. The Mallorca 312 isn't a race to win; it’s a ride to finish. There are no rankings, just personal milestones.

## The Takeaway

If you’re feeling uninspired by your own fitness routine, take a leaf out of the Hazard handbook. You don't need to be a pro to tackle something "impossible."

*   **Pick a goal that challenges you, not just your ego:** Hazard didn't choose the easiest route. He chose a distance that forced him to acknowledge his own limits. Find an event or a trail that feels slightly out of reach.
*   **Don't take yourself so seriously:** The "Quick" burger jersey proves you can be serious about your goals without losing your sense of humor. If you can’t laugh at yourself while you’re out there suffering, you’re missing half the fun.
*   **The "Recovery" is part of the reward:** While we shouldn't necessarily base our diets on beer and burgers, the sentiment remains: treat your achievements as moments of joy. Make sure you celebrate the effort, not just the result.

As the 16th edition of the Mallorca 312 approaches, people are already wondering if Hazard will return. Whether he loses those extra kilos or shows up with another burger-themed kit, he has already succeeded in doing something more important than winning a match: he reminded us that life is meant to be lived at our own pace—even if that pace includes a cold one at the finish line. 

Next time you finish a tough workout, keep this in mind: if Eden Hazard can pedal 167 kilometers through the mountains and laugh about the state of his legs, you can definitely handle whatever challenge you’ve set for yourself today. Just maybe skip the burger until *after* the ride.
