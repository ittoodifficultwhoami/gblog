It’s the conversation every parent dreads. Your daughter walks into the kitchen, eyes bright with excitement, and announces she’s set her heart on a prestigious—and likely very expensive—university. You’re already busy calculating the mortgage, your retirement, and the staggering reality of current tuition rates. (Honestly, I still break into a cold sweat thinking about what college will cost in ten years.)

You aren’t alone in this. When it comes to education, emotions and finances constantly collide. Don’t write a blank check or shut down the conversation entirely. Instead, move from "feeling" to "calculating." If your daughter is interested in business, you have a unique opportunity to turn this into her first real-world assignment.

Here is how to crunch the numbers, face the reality of student debt, and decide if a school is actually worth the price tag.

## The "Business Plan" for Her Education

![Simple cartoon illustration: A document labeled "ROI PLAN" featuring a pie chart showing a 60/40 split between "Debt" and "Future Salary."](/images/should-you-pay-for-that-expensive-college-a-roi-guide_0.png)

If she wants to study business, she needs to treat her college search like a business investment. Any smart entrepreneur knows that before you commit to a major expense, you calculate the Return on Investment (ROI).

Have her build a "Business Plan" for her degree. Forget about the campus "vibe" or where she wants to live for a second. Force her to look at these three things:

*   **The Debt Projection:** Exactly how much is she borrowing?
*   **The Salary Forecast:** What is the realistic starting salary for this specific major?
*   **The Breakeven Analysis:** How long does it actually take to pay off the balance?

### The Reality of Debt

Students constantly underestimate how interest rates destroy their math. For the 2025–2026 school year, federal undergraduate interest rates sit at **6.39%**. 

If she borrows $30,000, here is the real-world consequence:
*   **Monthly Payment:** About **$341**.
*   **Repayment Term:** 10 years.
*   **Total Cost:** She pays back roughly **$39,000**.

That’s $9,000 in interest—the price of a solid used car—just for the privilege of borrowing the money. Stick to this rule of thumb: her total student debt should never exceed her expected first-year starting salary. It’s a painful ceiling, but it keeps you out of financial ruin.

### A Case Study in Value

To see how this works, look at the Shidler College of Business at the University of Hawaiʻi at Mānoa. People often turn their noses up at state schools, but the data here is actually incredible.

Shidler holds the **AACSB accreditation**. That’s the "gold standard" of business education held by fewer than 6% of schools worldwide, and it currently ranks #20 nationally for International Business. 

For a student balancing quality with actual cost, the breakdown is eye-opening:

| Tuition Category | Annual Cost (2025-2026) |
| :--- | :--- |
| In-State (Resident) | $11,520 |
| Western Undergraduate Exchange (WUE) | $17,280 |
| Out-of-State (Standard) | $33,552 |

**Why this matters:** A student who qualifies for the WUE rate saves **$16,272 per year** compared to standard out-of-state tuition. If she’s a WUE student, her total annual bill comes out to about $38,838. That is a massive difference in her long-term financial health.

### The "Community College" Shortcut

![Simple cartoon illustration: A split path icon; one side has a heavy backpack labeled "Full Debt," the other side a light pack labeled "50% Off."](/images/should-you-pay-for-that-expensive-college-a-roi-guide_3.png)

If she’s dead set on a school like Mānoa but wants to keep her bank account intact, point her toward the **Kaʻieʻie Program**. She can knock out her first two years at a UH Community College and slash her tuition costs by about 50%. Honestly, it’s the smartest financial move she could make.

Think of it as the "stealth" route to a degree. She ends up with the exact same diploma as someone who spent four years at a university, just without the crushing debt. Does she really want to start her adult life staring down a massive student loan payment, or would she rather cross the finish line with a clean slate? 

## The Career Payoff

![Simple cartoon illustration: A bar chart showing salary bars; the tallest bar is labeled "Technical Skills" with a gold star icon.](/images/should-you-pay-for-that-expensive-college-a-roi-guide_4.png)

Not all degrees pay the same. Before she gets her heart set on a specific school, have her look at the projected starting salaries for these business majors:

*   **Business Analytics:** $75,000
*   **Finance:** $74,500
*   **Accounting:** $71,000
*   **General Business:** $65,276
*   **Marketing:** $60,000

If she picks a high-debt path for a lower-earning degree, those loans will hang over her head for a long time. It’s a classic trap. On the flip side, mastering technical skills like **SQL, Python, or Tableau** is a total game changer. It nets her a 10–20% salary premium, which is the fastest way to get out from under that debt.

## The "M7" Myth vs. Reality

![Simple cartoon illustration: A balance scale weighing a gold trophy against a bag of money, showing the money bag is much heavier.](/images/should-you-pay-for-that-expensive-college-a-roi-guide_5.png)

If your daughter has her heart set on an "M7" business school like Stanford or Wharton, she’s likely convinced it’s the only way to make it big. Those programs boast flashy compensation packages topping **$200,000**, but let’s be real: those grads are the outliers, not the baseline.

The "payback period"—the time it takes to earn back her tuition and those two years of lost salary—is usually **2 to 4 years**. That’s a brutal, high-pressure sprint. Unless she’s dead set on investment banking or private equity, where starting salaries hit **$90,000 to $125,000**, the massive price tag of an elite degree rarely translates to a higher lifetime income. Honestly, she’s probably better off focusing on the actual skills she'll pick up rather than the prestige on the diploma.

## Practical Takeaways: How to Have the Talk

![Simple cartoon illustration: A checklist icon with four checkmarks and a large magnifying glass hovering over a tuition document.](/images/should-you-pay-for-that-expensive-college-a-roi-guide_6.png)

Don’t lecture her; help her investigate. Use this roadmap for your next conversation:

1. **The Internship Factor:** Does the school actually place students in jobs? Shidler facilitates over 400 internships annually, and a third of those students get full-time offers from their employers. Honestly, that connection is worth way more than a school’s "name brand."

2. **Location Matters:** She should choose a school where she actually wants to live. Networking happens locally. If she wants to work in Honolulu, a degree from Shidler carries serious weight because the alumni network is packed with leaders at companies like Hawaiian Airlines, EY, and Deloitte.

3. **The FAFSA Deadline:** If she is looking at 2026, the FAFSA cycle opens on October 1, 2025. This is when the real money is handed out. Remind her that deadlines are part of business—missing one can cost her thousands.

4. **Explore Ancestry Benefits:** If you have Native Hawaiian ancestry, check if she qualifies for the resident tuition rate of $11,520. That is a massive financial benefit. Don’t leave that money on the table.

5. **Option A vs. Option B:** Have her build a spreadsheet with two columns: "Dream School" and "Value School." Calculate the 10-year total cost for both. Ask her, "What would you do with the $50,000–$100,000 you saved by picking the second option?"

You aren’t saying "no" to her dreams; you are saying "yes" to her future financial health. A degree is a tool, not a trophy. If she picks a school with strong accreditation, a history of internships, and a manageable tuition rate, she graduates with the freedom to choose her first job based on passion—not just the desperate need to pay off a loan.

Run the numbers together. Let her see the math. Once she owns the data, the decision becomes much clearer.
