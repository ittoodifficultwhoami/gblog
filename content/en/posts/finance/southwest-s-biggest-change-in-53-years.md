# Southwest Airlines Is Making Huge Changes — Here's What You Need to Know

Surprised by Southwest Airlines laying off employees for the first time ever?

 You're not alone. The airline built its reputation on friendly service, two free bags, and that unique open seating policy we all have opinions about. Now everything's changing. Fast. Here's what's happening at Southwest, why it matters for your travel plans, and where this iconic airline is headed next.

## Financial Performance: The Good News First

Southwest actually crushed it financially in 2024. Seriously:

- Record fourth quarter operating revenues of $6.9 billion
- Record full year operating revenues of $27.5 billion

![Simple cartoon illustration: Money bag with "$27.5B" and cartoon plane soaring upward](/images/southwest-s-biggest-change-in-53-years_1.png)
- Full year net income of $465 million ($0.76 per diluted share)
- When you exclude special items? Net income jumps to $597 million ($0.96 per diluted share)

CEO Bob Jordan shared some good news: "We closed out 2024 with positive momentum from our revenue initiatives and the performance of our operation. While we still have much work to do, we are pleased that the improvements from our tactical initiatives are materializing faster than expected."

The airline is sitting pretty with $9.7 billion in liquidity (way above their $6.7 billion in debt). They returned $680 million to shareholders in 2024 and just announced a $750 million accelerated share repurchase program. That's some serious financial firepower.

## Southwest 2.0: The Biggest Change in 53 Years

Get ready for a shock if you've been flying Southwest for years. They're launching what they call "Southwest. Even Better." – described as "the largest and most comprehensive transformational plan in our 53-year history."

Translation: everything you know about Southwest is about to change.

![Simple cartoon illustration: Southwest plane transforming, adding assigned seats and premium seating](/images/southwest-s-biggest-change-in-53-years_2.png)

- **Assigned seating**: Yes, really. After decades of bragging about their open seating model, Southwest is abandoning ship. This is HUGE for a airline that built part of its identity on this policy.

- **Premium seating options**: With assigned seating comes extra legroom seats you'll probably pay more for. Just like every other airline.

- **Enhanced co-brand credit card benefits**: Southwest and Chase are teaming up to give credit card holders better benefits, especially connected to the new assigned seating.

These changes are happening because Elliott Investment Management took a major stake in the carrier.

![Simple cartoon illustration: Big investor figure pushing Southwest plane toward "CHANGE" arrow](/images/southwest-s-biggest-change-in-53-years_3.png) In plain English? The investors saw ways to make more money and are pushing hard for these changes. Personally, I'm not shocked – open seating was becoming a relic in an industry where everyone else charges for better seats.

## Workforce Reductions: First-Ever Layoffs

This is the stunner. Southwest, famous for never laying off employees in its entire history, is cutting jobs:

![Simple cartoon illustration: "1,750 jobs cut" on sign with sad cartoon worker figures](/images/southwest-s-biggest-change-in-53-years_4.png)

- **1,750 jobs cut**: That's about 2.3% of their roughly 75,000 employees.
- **Focus on corporate and leadership roles**: At least your flight attendants and pilots aren't getting the boot.
- **Aim to save $300 million by 2026**: These layoffs are part of Southwest's plan to slash costs.
- **Includes senior leadership positions**: They're even cutting from the top – not just the lower-level positions.

The fact that Southwest is laying off anyone at all tells you how serious they are about these changes. Their employee-friendly culture was legendary. Now that's changing too.

## What's Driving These Changes?

Southwest was profitable in 2024, so why the dramatic shake-up? A few reasons:

1. **Investment pressure**: Elliott Investment Management didn't invest billions just to watch Southwest keep doing business as usual. They want changes to boost profits.

2. **Post-pandemic industry shifts**: The entire airline industry is still figuring out new travel patterns, fuel costs, and what customers want after the pandemic.

3. **Competitive pressures**: While Southwest was holding onto open seating, other airlines were cashing in on assigned seats and premium options.

4. **Future-focused strategy**: Southwest is looking ahead, not just at next quarter. They're trying to build an airline that thrives long-term.

## What These Changes Mean for Passengers

Enough about the business stuff. What does this mean for you?

### The Seating Revolution

Southwest's open seating system was something people either loved or hated. Some enjoyed choosing their own seats, others found the chaotic boarding process stressful.

With assigned seats coming:

![Simple cartoon illustration: Relaxed passenger with numbered seat vs stressed passenger in boarding line](/images/southwest-s-biggest-change-in-53-years_5.png)

- **No more boarding position stress**: You'll know exactly where you're sitting before you even get to the airport.
- **Potential for additional revenue**: Expect to pay extra for "better" seats, just like other airlines.
- **End of the "cattle call" feeling**: Many travelers will appreciate not having to line up early just to get a decent seat.

I'll be honest – as someone who always stressed about getting a good boarding position, I'm kind of relieved.

### Potential Changes to Baggage Policies

There are whispers about baggage changes too:

![Simple cartoon illustration: Worried passenger with two bags looking at "POSSIBLE FEE?" sign](/images/southwest-s-biggest-change-in-53-years_6.png)

- **New carry-on bag requirement in 2026**: Forum discussions suggest Southwest might add size restrictions for carry-on bags.
- **Will the famous "two free bags" continue?**: This has been Southwest's signature benefit, so changing it would be a massive deal.

### Loyalty Program Considerations

If you're a Southwest frequent flyer:

- **Basic fare booked with points**: Customers are confused about how Basic fare tickets work when booked with points.
- **A-List status value**: Some are wondering if earning A-List status in 2026 will be worth it with all these changes.
- **Credit card benefits**: The new Chase partnership means potential improvements to Southwest credit card offerings.

## Southwest's Financial Strategy

Beyond what passengers see, Southwest is making serious financial moves:

- **Cost reduction target**: They're pushing to beat their 2027 goal of $500 million in cost cuts.
- **Shareholder returns**: They're giving money back to shareholders through dividends and stock buybacks.
- **Strong liquidity position**: With $9.7 billion in cash versus $6.7 billion in debt, Southwest isn't going anywhere.

This is all about making Wall Street happy while keeping the airline strong enough to survive whatever hits next.

## What's Next for Southwest?

2025 will be a "pivotal year" according to CEO Bob Jordan. They'll roll out these massive changes while trying to keep their operations running smoothly and customers happy.

Watch for:

- **When assigned seating starts**: The exact timing will matter a lot to travelers.
- **Customer reaction**: Will Southwest's loyal fans embrace these changes or rebel?
- **Financial impact**: Will investors get the returns they're pushing for?
- **Employee morale**: After those first-ever layoffs, how does Southwest maintain its famously positive culture?

## The Bottom Line for Travelers

So what should you actually do about all this?

![Simple cartoon illustration: Confused passenger looking at choice between "OLD Southwest" and "NEW Southwest"](/images/southwest-s-biggest-change-in-53-years_7.png)

**If you're a Southwest customer:**
- Stay informed about when assigned seating happens and how it affects your boarding process.
- If you're a Rapid Rewards member, watch for changes to the loyalty program and credit card benefits.
- Enjoy those two free checked bags while they last – but keep an eye out for policy changes.
- Reconsider if Southwest is still your best airline choice once these changes kick in.

**If you're considering Southwest for the first time:**
- Don't write them off just yet. Southwest still has advantages, especially with baggage policies and no change fees.
- Wait and see how their new seating system works before judging – many travelers will actually prefer it.
- Compare total value: price, baggage policies, flight schedules, and the whole experience.

Southwest is transforming, but they're still committed to low-cost, friendly air travel. These changes are meant to strengthen the airline financially while still delivering value to customers.

The airline industry never stops changing. Southwest's decision to adapt instead of staying the same shows they understand that. The layoffs and policy shifts might seem dramatic, but they're calculated moves for long-term success, not desperate survival tactics.

Expect competitive prices and friendly service to continue – just with some new twists as Southwest joins the modern airline era.
