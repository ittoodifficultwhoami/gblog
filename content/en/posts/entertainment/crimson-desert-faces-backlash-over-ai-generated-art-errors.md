Pearl Abyss just sold 2 million copies of **Crimson Desert** in 24 hours, but they have a massive problem on their hands. Players are hunting down obvious AI-generated art hidden throughout the game, and the community is furious. If you feel like you are being sold a premium experience only to find low-effort mistakes, you are right to be annoyed. This is a masterclass in how to torch player trust.

## The evidence of AI in the wild

The backlash is not based on rumors. Players are finding glaring anatomical errors that are classic signatures of generative AI. 

At Oakenshield Manor in Hernand City, a large portrait features horses with five legs and warriors with melted, mushy facial features. Over at Marni’s House, woodcut-style art shows characters with impossible finger counts—three on one hand, four on the other. 

These are not artistic choices. They are errors caused by AI models failing to understand basic biology or physics, and they contradict everything the marketing team promised.

## The handcrafted promise vs. the reality

Before launch, Marketing Director Will Powers went on record to promise a premium, handcrafted experience. He specifically touted human-made quality, including 100% human voice acting. Pearl Abyss invested 30 billion won—roughly $26 million—into an Art Center designed specifically for motion capture and 3D scanning.

When a studio spends that much on human craftsmanship and then ships art that clearly came from a cheap generator, it creates a massive trust gap. Players feel lied to, not just by the quality of the art, but by the marketing that positioned the game as a high-end, artisanal product. Personally, I would rather see a blank wall than a five-legged horse in a game that claims to be a masterpiece.

## Why this matters for Steam policy

Valve updated its AI disclosure rules on January 16, 2026. These rules are strict: if you use AI to create assets that the player sees, you must disclose it on the Steam store page.

As of March 21, 2026, the **Crimson Desert** store page has zero mentions of AI-generated content. By failing to disclose these assets, Pearl Abyss is walking on thin ice with Valve. If Steam decides to enforce its policy, it could lead to penalties, store visibility issues, or forced updates, especially given the game's high profile.

## The placeholder trap: AI vs. MS Paint

Some defenders claim this is just a case of forgotten placeholder art. Even if a developer forgot to swap a test file, using AI as a placeholder is a massive strategic blunder.

**AI-Generated Placeholders**
*   **Perception:** Viewed as deceptive and low-effort.
*   **Visibility:** Because AI aims for realism, errors like five-legged horses stand out as jarring and uncanny.
*   **Trust:** Damages the "handcrafted" branding of the game.

**Human-Made Placeholders (e.g., Slay the Spire 2)**
*   **Perception:** Viewed as charming, honest, and humble.
*   **Visibility:** Using an MS Paint scribble with a label like "Painting" is obviously fake and creates no confusion.
*   **Trust:** Shows the developer is transparent about work-in-progress content.

When you ship an AI asset by mistake, you get a "mushy" face that ruins immersion. When you ship a literal MS Paint placeholder, you get a laugh and a nod of respect for the process.

## The website experience adds insult to injury

Beyond the game itself, players are frustrated with the promotional website. Users on Reddit describe the user experience as aggressive, citing a layout that prioritizes ads over actual information. This ad density feels insulting to a community that already feels the game’s internal assets are low-quality. When the surrounding marketing infrastructure feels cheap, players are much less forgiving when they spot "slop" inside the game.

## What is next for Pearl Abyss

The studio officially promised to make improvements following the mixed feedback on Steam. However, the damage to their reputation is already done. This appears to be a shift in the corporate pipeline; Pearl Abyss subsidiary CCP Games has already been linked to an AI collaboration with Elon Musk as of September 2025. 

## Practical takeaways for the player

If you want to navigate this mess, keep these points in mind:

*   **Watch for store page updates:** Check if Pearl Abyss adds an AI disclosure to the Steam page. If they do, they are acknowledging the tech. If they do not, they are choosing to ignore the rules, which tells you everything you need to know about their commitment to transparency.
*   **Value the honest placeholder:** The next time a developer uses crude, hand-drawn placeholder art, recognize it for what it is: an honest admission of a game still in progress. It is a sign of a developer that cares more about function than trying to hide a shortcut.
*   **Don't rely on marketing claims:** Marketing directors are paid to sell you a vision, not the technical reality of the asset folder. When a game touts a handcrafted experience, look for the signs of automation—weird fingers or blurry, nonsensical textures—before you click buy.
*   **Vote with your attention:** If a studio is not being honest about how they build their games, they lose the right to the benefit of the doubt. Give your time and money to developers who own their mistakes and prioritize the player experience over hidden shortcuts.
