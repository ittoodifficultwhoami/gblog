The FBI is buying your location history, and they have no intention of stopping.

If you thought your phone’s location data was protected by the Fourth Amendment, you are right—but that protection only applies if the government asks your phone carrier for it. By paying private data brokers for that same information, the FBI has found a legal shortcut that bypasses the courts entirely. They are using your tax dollars to track your daily movements without a judge signing a single warrant.

## How the loophole works

In 2018, the Supreme Court ruled in **Carpenter v. United States** that the government needs a warrant to get location data from cell phone companies. This only shifted the problem to the private market. 

The FBI stopped going to your wireless provider and started going to data brokers like **Babel Street**, the company behind a tool called **Locate X**. These brokers scrape location pings generated by almost every app on your phone, from weather trackers to casual games. These apps create roughly 8,000 bid requests per person, every month. Brokers bundle this data, clean it up, and sell it to whoever pays. 

Because the FBI buys this on the open market, they claim the Fourth Amendment does not apply. FBI Director Kash Patel confirmed this in March 2026, stating that if a marketing firm can buy your data, the government should be able to as well. It is a cynical take, but it is the current reality.

## The new era of Agentic AI

The danger is the speed of this surveillance. As of early 2026, brokers use **Agentic AI** to analyze this data.

Surveillance used to require slow, manual labor. Now, automated systems cross-correlate anonymous data pings with your home address and social media profiles in real time. You are no longer just being watched if you are a suspect; you are part of a continuous data stream that the government can query whenever they feel like it.

## Surveillance for thee, but not for me

The irony is hard to miss. While the FBI spends millions of tax dollars to track average citizens, agency leadership is being watched by the very same technologies they use on the public.

Director Kash Patel faced backlash after reports showed him using a $60 million government **Gulfstream G550** jet to transport his girlfriend to social events and the Olympics. Private citizens tracked Patel’s movements using publicly available flight data, proving that the surveillance state is a one-way mirror. The government wants to track you for security, but they clearly don't like the feeling of being watched themselves.

## The current state of play

| Feature | The Old Way (Pre-2018) | The New Way (2026) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Legal Hurdle** | Warrant required for carrier data | None (Open market purchase) |
| **Technology** | Manual request to telecom | Automated Agentic AI profiling |
| **Source** | Phone network logs | Ad-tech and app location pings |
| **Frequency** | Targeted/Reactive | Mass/Proactive |

The FBI’s contract with Babel Street is worth roughly **$27 million**. You are effectively funding the system that monitors your own life.

## What happens next

Congress is trying to close this gap. The **Government Surveillance Reform Act (GSRA)** aims to mandate warrants for all commercial data purchases by intelligence agencies. It is the main battleground for anyone who believes privacy should not be a commodity the government can just buy off a shelf.

Progress is slow. The Federal Trade Commission banned brokers like **Venntel** from selling data regarding visits to sensitive locations like clinics, but the "national security" loopholes remain wide open.

## What you can do

The legal system is lagging, so you have to protect yourself by changing your digital habits.

* **Audit your app permissions:** Check your phone settings. Most apps—especially flashlights, weather, or simple games—do not need constant location access. Change these to "Only While Using" or "Never."
* **Limit background data:** Disable background app refresh for apps that do not need to track you when they are closed.
* **Support privacy legislation:** Contact your representatives and ask where they stand on the **Government Surveillance Reform Act**. Politicians only pay attention when they realize their constituents are watching.
* **Stop assuming you are anonymous:** When your location is cross-referenced with your home address and digital footprint, you are not anonymous. Stop letting apps sell the map of your life to the highest bidder.

The FBI is not going to stop buying this data. As long as it is for sale and the law stays murky, your movements will be treated as a product. Secure your settings and push for the oversight required to finally force the government to get a warrant before they start following you.
