### What is Ranked-Choice Voting, Anyway?

![Simple cartoon illustration: A flowchart showing a ballot ranking 1st, 2nd, and 3rd choices, with an arrow pointing to a '50% + 1' goal circle.](/images/alaska-s-ranked-choice-voting-faces-new-repeal-effort_0.png)

To understand the fight over Ranked Choice Voting, we first need to see how the system actually works. Traditional elections are straightforward: you pick one person, and whoever gets the most votes wins, even if they didn't even crack 50%. Ranked Choice Voting (RCV) flips the script by asking you to rank candidates in order of preference.

The goal is to make sure the winner has genuine, broad support. To clinch a victory, a candidate needs **50% + 1** of the vote. If nobody hits that mark in the first round, the candidate with the fewest votes gets the boot. Crucially, the people who voted for that loser don't just get ignored; their votes shift to their second choice. This repeats until someone finally crosses the 50% line. Honestly, it’s a lot more logical than our current winner-take-all mess.

Supporters say this forces candidates to appeal to a wider range of voters instead of just pandering to their radical base. Critics, though, think the whole thing is a confusing headache. They point to "exhausted ballots"—those are ballots that stop counting because every single candidate a voter ranked got eliminated in earlier rounds. 

### The Rise of the Repeal Effort

![Simple cartoon illustration: A large pile of 42,837 paper documents being checked off against a map showing all 40 districts shaded in blue.](/images/alaska-s-ranked-choice-voting-faces-new-repeal-effort_1.png)

Alaska adopted ranked-choice voting and open primaries through a ballot measure in 2020. Since then, the system has faced constant pushback. Donald Trump notoriously trashed it as "ranked choice crap voting" at an Anchorage rally back in 2022, and he reignited that fight in April 2026 by calling the system "one of the greatest threats to democracy" on social media. Honestly, he’s never been one to mince words.

Trump isn’t acting alone. He’s joined by heavy hitters in the Alaska GOP, including Governor Mike Dunleavy, Senator Dan Sullivan, Representative Nick Begich, and party chair Carmela Warfield. These leaders argue that returning to the old way of doing things is the only path to "TRUE 'Democracy'." Their new repeal initiative is pretty straightforward: it aims to kill ranked-choice voting, scrap the "top-four" open primary system, and axe the campaign finance disclosure rules established in 2020.

The repeal movement has real momentum. After submitting signatures on November 6, 2025, the campaign had 42,837 signatures verified by Lt. Gov. Nancy Dahlstrom by the end of the year. That easily cleared the 34,098-signature requirement. Even more impressive for their side, they met the geographic distribution rules in all 40 of Alaska’s House districts—well beyond the 30 required.

### A Closer Look: The 2024 "Dry Run"

![Simple cartoon illustration: A tight tug-of-war rope between two groups, with the scoreboard showing 160,973 vs 160,230 and a small '743' gap.](/images/alaska-s-ranked-choice-voting-faces-new-repeal-effort_2.png)

This isn't the first time the system has been tested. Back in 2024, voters faced Ballot Measure 2, which aimed to repeal RCV. The result was a nail-biter, proving just how split the state really is.

*   **Total "No" (Keep RCV) votes:** 160,973 (50.11%)
*   **Total "Yes" (Repeal) votes:** 160,230 (49.89%)
*   **The Margin:** A razor-thin **743 votes** after a statewide recount. Honestly, that’s a terrifyingly small gap for such a big decision.

The geographic split here tells the real story. Alaska Native districts held the line. In districts 38, 39, and 40, the "No" vote dominated with a 2,960-vote lead. Looking at District 40—the Arctic and North Slope—61% of people chose to keep the system.

The "No" side had a massive financial edge, raking in about $14 million. Even with that kind of money behind them, the repeal movement lost by less than one percentage point. This near-miss has convinced repeal advocates they’re just one good campaign away from victory in 2026.

### The Legal and Legislative Backdrop

![Simple cartoon illustration: A side-by-side comparison table icon showing a person with one checkmark vs a person with a ranked list of four.](/images/alaska-s-ranked-choice-voting-faces-new-repeal-effort_3.png)

The battle over elections isn’t just happening at the ballot box—it’s playing out in the courtroom, too. Both sides of the 2026 repeal initiative filed lawsuits this February, mainly picking apart the specific wording voters will see on the ballot. This isn't exactly new territory, either. Back in August 2024, the Alaska Supreme Court had to step in just to keep the original initiative from being kicked off the ballot entirely.

The pushback is hitting the federal level now, too. In April 2025, Representative Nick Begich co-introduced the "Preventing Ranked Choice Corruption Act." The goal is simple: ban ranked-choice voting in federal elections nationwide. Honestly, it’s a massive pivot that signals just how polarizing this system has become.
