Stop talking about people’s bodies. Even if you think you are being kind, you are crossing a line that has serious consequences for the other person’s health and mental well-being. Practicing **Body Silence** is the most respectful thing you can do for the people around you.

Minding your own business is a public health intervention. When you comment on a body, you have no idea what that person is enduring. You might be triggering an eating disorder, insulting a grieving parent, or stressing someone out so much that their body enters a defensive, high-stress state.

### The science proves your comments backfire

For years, people claimed that discussing weight was a way to offer encouragement. That is scientifically false. Shaming people about their weight does not lead to health. It leads to weight gain.

When someone feels judged for their size, their body triggers the **HPA axis** and causes a spike in **cortisol**. Higher cortisol levels increase the hunger hormone, **ghrelin**, and promote the storage of **visceral fat**. The pressure you apply is biologically working against the health goals you think you are supporting. People who internalize weight stigma are **three times as likely** to develop metabolic syndrome, regardless of what the scale says. 

### You are operating on incomplete information

You never know the story behind the body you are looking at. 

*   **Eating Disorder Triggers:** Between **20% and 50%** of people recovering from an eating disorder will experience a relapse. When you tell a friend they look like they have lost weight, you are validating the behaviors of an illness that might be starving them. 
*   **The Reproductive Minefield:** Approximately **1 in 8** known pregnancies end in miscarriage. When you ask a woman if she is pregnant, you are potentially interrogating someone about a recent, painful loss. **41.3%** of women struggling with infertility suffer from symptoms of PTSD. An innocent question about a baby bump can be a traumatic violation of a private medical reality.
*   **The Workplace Bias:** Your comments have real-world consequences. **45% of employers** admit they are less likely to hire a candidate with obesity, and women with higher BMIs face an average **8% to 10% wage penalty**. By participating in body surveillance, you are fueling a culture that hurts people's ability to earn a living.

### Compliments are not neutral

A compliment about someone’s size is really about your own standards for what a body should look like. When you tell someone they look great because they lost weight, you reinforce the idea that their value is tied to their size. You are also telling them that their previous body was worse. 

### Comparison: The old way vs. the new standard

| Traditional Social Habit | The Reality | Why we need Body Silence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Complimenting weight loss | Reinforces the idea that thin is the only good state. | It can trigger relapse for those in ED recovery. |
| Asking when are you due? | It is an inquiry into a private medical condition. | It risks re-traumatizing people struggling with infertility. |
| Tough love or shaming | It is linked to a 6.67 times greater risk of obesity. | Shame causes cortisol spikes that promote fat storage. |
| Workplace banter | 21% of Gen Z workers feel judged at work. | It fosters bias and reduces professional equity. |

### How to adopt Body Silence today

You do not have to be cold to practice this. You just need to find more interesting things to talk about.

**1. Scrub your vocabulary**
Stop using weight, size, or shape as a shorthand for health or happiness. If you want to compliment a friend, focus on their actions, their humor, or their kindness. If you find yourself wanting to comment on a change in someone's body, pause. Is this necessary? Is this kind? Is this any of your business?

**2. Stop the cycle of surveillance**
If someone else starts body-shaming a third party, do not join in. You do not need to be a jerk to stop it. A simple, neutral, I don't really have an opinion on their body is enough to signal that you are not playing that game.

**3. Recognize the invisible**
Assume everyone you meet is dealing with something you cannot see. You are not a doctor, and you are not entitled to a medical report on the people you encounter. 

**4. Respect the boundaries of others**
If someone mentions their own diet, you do not have to mirror the conversation. Redirect the talk toward how they are feeling or what they are excited about. 

Your interactions should leave people feeling seen for who they are, not for how they look. **Body Silence** is about respecting others enough to leave their physical form out of the conversation. It is a simple, free way to make your community a safer place for everyone.
