Every week, there’s a new study claiming something in your kitchen—or the air you breathe—is sabotaging your health. If you’re feeling overwhelmed by this endless parade of "hidden dangers," you aren’t alone. It’s exhausting to navigate a world where common household items carry long-term risks that don't show up until decades later. Honestly, I think we’re all a little burnt out on the fear-mongering.

But today, we’re looking at a chemical that’s actually different. It isn’t just another headline about a suspicious food additive; it’s a heavy-duty industrial solvent that has been circulating in our environment for over a century. It’s called **Trichloroethylene (TCE)**. After decades of research, scientists have confirmed a chilling link between this chemical and an increased risk of Parkinson’s disease.

### What is TCE, and Why Was It Everywhere?

![Simple cartoon illustration: A retro-style aerosol can with a label reading "INDUSTRIAL SOLVENT" pouring liquid onto a greasy, dirty gear.](/images/tce-the-common-cleaner-linked-to-parkinson-s-disease-risk_0.png)



![Simple cartoon illustration: A worried stick figure character staring at a mountain of household spray bottles with oversized red warning icons.](/images/tce-the-common-cleaner-linked-to-parkinson-s-disease-risk_1.png)

Trichloroethylene (CAS Number 79-01-6) sounds like a dense lab chemical, but for most of the 20th century, it was a household workhorse. Because it’s an aggressive solvent that melts through grease, oils, and adhesives, it became a staple in the American home. 

You’ve probably seen it under old trade names like **Tri-Clene, Trilene, or Sprayway C-60**. Historically, TCE was everywhere:

*   **Aerosol degreasers:** Brake and engine cleaners.
*   **Household cleaning:** Spot removers for clothes and upholstery.
*   **Adhesives:** Spray glues and sealants.
*   **Office supplies:** Original typewriter correction fluids.

It was so pervasive that manufacturers used it to decaffeinate coffee and even as an anesthetic. It’s wild to think people were inhaling that stuff in a doctor’s office. The FDA finally banned those specific uses in 1977, but by then, the chemical was already baked into the environment. 

### The Parkinson’s Connection: A 500% Increased Risk

![Simple cartoon illustration: A stylized human brain outline showing a glowing "danger zone" at the base with a 510% growth arrow pointing up.](/images/tce-the-common-cleaner-linked-to-parkinson-s-disease-risk_2.png)

TCE is terrifying not just because it’s toxic, but because of how it sneaks into your brain. Once it enters your body, it crosses the blood-brain barrier with ease. It goes straight for the substantia nigra—the engine room for dopamine production. From there, it causes mitochondrial dysfunction, essentially starving your neurons of energy until they die. This is the biological trigger behind the tremors and movement issues that define Parkinson’s disease. Honestly, it’s a brutal way to dismantle the nervous system.

The data is impossible to ignore:

*   **The Twin Study (2011):** A landmark study in the *Annals of Neurology* followed pairs of twins to see how environmental factors changed their health. Those with occupational or hobby-related TCE exposure were 6.1 times more likely to develop Parkinson’s than their unexposed siblings.
*   **The Veteran Experience (2023):** Researchers looked at 340,489 veterans, specifically comparing those stationed at Camp Lejeune—where the water was famously contaminated—to those at clean bases. The contaminated group had a 70% higher risk of the disease.
*   **Environmental Exposure:** You don’t need to handle this stuff directly to be at risk. An October 2025 study from the Barrow Neurological Institute found a 10% increased risk for people living in ZIP codes with the highest outdoor concentrations of TCE.

### The "Silent" Threat: Latency and Contamination

![Simple cartoon illustration: A cross-section diagram of a house sitting above a plume of toxic chemicals in the ground, shown as a blue cloud.](/images/tce-the-common-cleaner-linked-to-parkinson-s-disease-risk_3.png)

You might be wondering why we’re only hearing about this now. The answer is a brutal 10 to 40-year latency period. People exposed to TCE back in the 80s or 90s are often only developing Parkinson’s symptoms today. It’s a terrifyingly slow fuse.

The contamination is rarely obvious, too. Take Endicott, New York. For years, an IBM facility there used TCE to manufacture parts. The chemical leaked into the groundwater, creating a 300-acre plume that stretched all the way to the Susquehanna River. Because the chemical evaporates, it turned into toxic vapors that seeped right into the basements of nearby homes. This "vapor intrusion" was so bad that over 450 properties needed specialized ventilation systems just to keep the air inside those houses breathable. Imagine finding out your own basement is a gas chamber.

The scale of this is hard to wrap your head around, so look at the numbers:

*   **The EPA’s limit:** The U.S. Maximum Contaminant Level for TCE in drinking water is 5 parts per billion (ppb).
*   **The reality:** At Camp Lejeune’s Hadnot Point, researchers recorded levels of 18,000 ppb in February 1985. Endicott, NY, saw historical groundwater levels hit 15,000 ppb.

### Is It Still in Our Homes?

![Simple cartoon illustration: A calendar graphic with a bold red "X" over the year 2025 and a small text box reading "EPA BAN EFFECTIVE NOW."](/images/tce-the-common-cleaner-linked-to-parkinson-s-disease-risk_4.png)

The tide is finally turning, even if it took its sweet time. While the UK and EU phased out industrial use of TCE back in April 2016, the United States only caught up recently. 

On December 17, 2024, the U.S. EPA finalized a sweeping rule under the Toxic Substances Control Act to ban nearly all uses of TCE. A temporary stay was lifted by the Third Circuit Court of Appeals on March 28, 2025, which finally cleared the way for a total phase-out of consumer products containing the chemical. Honestly, it’s about time—nobody wants this stuff in their cleaning supplies.

There are a few narrow exceptions. The EPA still permits specific uses in essential laboratories and for environmental cleanup efforts. Additionally, manufacturers have until September 15, 2028, to phase out its use in nuclear fuel production.

### How to Protect Yourself

If you’ve spent years working with industrial degreasers or solvents, your risk is massive. You need to be proactive about your health screenings. 

For those who lived in areas with contaminated water supplies, the danger is real, even if the percentage is lower than direct occupational exposure. Don't brush it off. Talk to your doctor about your history; honestly, most primary care physicians won't connect these dots unless you bring it up yourself. 

Living in an area with high ambient air pollution adds a small, constant baseline of risk. You can't control the air outside your front door, but staying informed helps you make better decisions about where you live or how you filter your home environment. Take this data seriously and advocate for your own medical oversight.

![Simple cartoon illustration: A person holding a bucket, heading toward a facility labeled "Hazardous Waste" to safely dispose of old chemicals.](/images/tce-the-common-cleaner-linked-to-parkinson-s-disease-risk_6.png)

Reading about "toxic legacies" like TCE feels helpless. You can’t go back and change where you lived twenty years ago or what chemicals were floating around your old office. But you can take charge of your future right now.

1. **Clear Out Your Garage:** Dig out those crusty, decades-old cans of brake cleaner, aerosol degreasers, or spot removers. **Get rid of them.** Don’t toss them in the regular trash or pour them down the sink—that’s just asking for trouble. Look up your local "Hazardous Household Waste" collection days instead. It’s a pain, I know, but it’s worth the trip.
2. **Audit Your Cleaning Supplies:** The EPA finally banned TCE, so new products are safe. But if you’re a fan of "vintage" or weird, unbranded solvent cleaners, check the label. If you see "Trichloroethylene" or "TCE," stop using it immediately. Toss it. 
3. **Know Your Environment:** If you live near an old industrial site or a "Superfund" zone, look up the history on the EPA’s website. These areas often have public reports detailing exactly what’s in the groundwater. If you’re in a vapor intrusion area, make sure your home has proper radon or vapor mitigation systems. It’s better to be safe than sorry.
4. **Advocate for Transparency:** Parkinson's is complex, involving everything from genetics to lifestyle to the environment. Supporting research into toxic chemicals is the best way to make sure we aren't blindsided by the next invisible disaster.

You cannot fix the past, but you can control your present. The news about TCE is sobering, but the fact that it’s finally banned is a massive win for public health. By being picky about the chemicals you bring into your home and keeping an eye on your neighborhood’s history, you cut down your exposure to these threats. Don’t live in fear. Just stay sharp.
