The most important thing you can do for the people in your life is remarkably simple: mind your own business.

Stop commenting on bodies. This applies to the ones you think you are complimenting and the ones you find confusing. Even if you think you are being supportive, your words carry weight you cannot see and history you do not know.

## The unintended cost of your words

Saying "You look great, have you lost weight?" is not a harmless way to make someone feel good. A 2024 University of Bristol study found that weight teasing at age 13 predicts higher internalized weight stigma and poorer mental health twenty years later.

When you comment on someone’s weight loss, you risk validating a life-threatening eating disorder. A friend who has lost a lot of weight may be in the middle of a health crisis, an undiagnosed medical condition, or intense grief. By calling that change a compliment, you tell them that their current, dangerous state is the version of them you prefer. 

Weight stigma causes real damage:

*   **Anxiety and Depression:** College students experiencing weight stigma are significantly more likely to report moderate-to-severe symptoms of anxiety (34 percent) and depression (35 percent).
*   **Eating Disorder Triggers:** Adolescents teased about their weight are nearly three times more likely to engage in binge eating.
*   **The Social Media Factor:** For every additional hour a teenager spends on social media, their risk of being bullied about their weight increases by 13 percent.

## The myth of the pregnancy question

"When are you due?" is a question that needs to be retired from your vocabulary. 

Medical conditions like **endometriosis** can cause what is often called **endo belly**, where the abdomen swells significantly due to internal inflammation. This has nothing to do with pregnancy, yet those suffering from it are frequently subjected to invasive, deeply painful questions from strangers and acquaintances. 

Approximately 1 in 6 people globally are affected by infertility. When you ask someone about a potential pregnancy, you are not engaging in small talk. You are poking at a wound related to pregnancy loss or medical struggle. It is a violation of privacy disguised as politeness.

## The legal shift

Body commentary is moving from a social faux pas to a civil rights issue. 

In November 2023, New York City implemented **Local Law 61**. This legislation makes it illegal to discriminate based on height or weight in employment, housing, and public accommodations. The legal landscape is catching up to the reality that a person's physical appearance is not a public project or an invitation for feedback. It is a protected aspect of their identity.

## Body neutrality vs. body positivity

The conversation has shifted from the body positivity movement of the 2010s to a more grounded approach called **body neutrality**. 

Body positivity encourages you to love your body exactly as it is. Body neutrality suggests that you do not have to love your body to respect it. It frames your body as a functional vessel—it is the thing that breathes, moves, and allows you to experience the world. Your body is not an aesthetic display piece for others to evaluate. 

Use the **five-second rule** of etiquette: only comment on a physical trait if the person can fix it in five seconds or less. Spinach in their teeth? Say something. A tear in their shirt? Say something. If it is something they cannot change in five seconds, keep it to yourself. Personally, I find this rule saves me from awkward foot-in-mouth moments constantly.

## How to handle the transition

If you have habitually commented on people's bodies, you may feel like you are losing a tool for connection. You are. But you are losing a hollow tool and gaining the ability to connect on a deeper level.

If you are receiving these comments, set a hard boundary. You are not obligated to be polite when someone violates your space. If someone asks if you are pregnant, or comments on your weight loss, shut it down. A simple, "I am not comfortable discussing my body" is a complete sentence. If they push, you do not owe them an explanation or a smile.

### Practical steps for moving forward:

*   **Audit your conversations:** Before you speak, ask yourself if the comment is about something they can change in five seconds. If the answer is no, stay silent.
*   **Replace body talk:** If you feel the urge to comment on someone's appearance, pivot to their actions or their energy. "It is so good to see you," or "You have been handling that project at work with so much focus," are genuine, human-centered observations.
*   **Recognize the medical reality:** Remind yourself that you do not know the medical history of the person standing in front of you. Assume there is a complex, sensitive story behind their physical appearance and treat them with the respect that implies.
*   **Protect your own peace:** If you are the target of body commentary, remember that their words reflect their lack of boundaries, not your worth. You are allowed to walk away, change the subject, or explicitly state that the topic is off-limits.

Your value is in your character and your actions, not in the shape, size, or appearance of your body. Choosing to stop commenting on bodies is a small, daily act of kindness that creates a safer, more respectful world.
